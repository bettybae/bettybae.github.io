PARIS, January 5, 1940

MINISTER OF EDUCATION
to Messrs. the Academic Inspectors

The Ministry of Armements is trying to resupply its factories, which absorb huge amounts of iron, at the lowest possible cost. The ore also requires, for the extraction of iron, large quantities of coke.

In order to avoid massive purchases of coal and iron abroad we thought of using the significant pure iron reserves that constitutes our old scrap metal.

The collection will be held in public by the mayors, who will receive from Messrs. the Prefects the necessary instructions. In principle, this will mean bringing the waste iron objects to the nearest train station.

I thought the school could have its role to play in this measure of national defense which all the French are invited [to participate in].

On the one hand, the teachers will show children the essential role of steel in modern warfare, the utility of iron for steelmaking, savings that the country can achieve using its old scrap metal.

They will also explain to their young students the collection mechanism and, where applicable, the profit that could be gained from the sale of their old iron objects for the families who would possess in abundant reserves (at a rate of 15 Frs. per 100 kg). -

Children will ensure the safest and most persuasive way the propaganda that does not easily extend to rural villages. They will also carry all the light objects to the collection centers, among which will normally include the town hall and school.

So I ask you to familiarize the teachers about the importance of this campaign, which should be started immediately and [done with] effective dedication. You will please communicate the attached outline of a lesson plan they will adapt to the various classes, at times developing more widely the scientific and technological aspects.

You will not fail to communicate to M. the Prefect the terms of this circular, and ask how your participation could be the most useful to him. -

Yvon DELBOS

IRON & STEEL IN MODERN WAR - THE COLLECTION OF OLD IRON OBJECTS

-: -: -: -: -: -: -: -: -: -: -: -: -

I. - ARMS, in the defensive as in the offensive, saving human lives threatened by the opposing arms. Almost all this armament consists of steel.

Land Force. Tanks. Guns and rifles. Machine guns. Ammunition.
Air Force. Aircraft - Engines. Guns and machine guns. Shells and bombs.
Sea Force. Battleships, Guns. Ammunition. Torpedoes. Machine tools.

The greater the equipment, the better that human life can be spared.

II. - WHERE DOES THE STEEL COME FROM? It is iron prepared in a certain way.
WHERE DOES THE IRON COME FROM? From ore processed with coke.
In higher level classes, you can take this opportunity to remind [the students] about the metallurgy of iron and steel) .-

III. What are OUR NATIONAL RESOURCES of iron ore & coal.

(Depending on the class, you can emphasize or slightly pass over this economic geography lesson) .-
Our wartime needs are much greater than in peacetime.
Importing iron, importing coke, means foreign purchases with gold, means weakening the country.
Collecting abandoned iron reserves, without use, is to, therefore, save the gold for the country.

IV. - SCRAP METAL. - In France, it has never been systematically recovered. And yet we export 500,000 tons annually. It consists of an old bucket, an old cookingpot, a jug with holes, cans, old children’s toys; old kitchen utensils, stoves, cookers, gas stoves or coal; old tools: broken pitchforks, worn plowshares, rusty shovels; old iron beds, etc. ......
All these iron objects save iron ore and coal.
We must gather them.

V - ROLE OF CHILDREN

1st - Propaganda in families.
2nd - Search for old objects,
3rd - Transport light objects.
4th - Spiritual Role: Safegaurding soldiers [who are] better armed, better protected; Safeguarding civilians against air attacks; a shortened war.

Identical copy
St. Brieux, January 8, 1940

Primary Inspector
in charge of the inspection service a/cademic (1st degree)

Lapeyrière March 4, 1940

Mrs. Lucienne Mazens

Teacher at Lapeyrière (Lisle s/ Tarn)
to Mr. Primary School Inspector

Inspector,

I have the honor to inform you that: following the ministerial circular of January 10, 1940, about the salvage of scrap metal, I made a point to respond by encouraging students to look for old iron objects.

And here are the comments that were addressed to me, Sunday, March 3, 1940, at the tobacco shop in the hamlet and in front of several people, by somebody who was temporarily discharged: Mr. Vilette, property-owner in Lapeyrière (Lisle s/ Tarn) and father of a student:

- “I do not want my son to collect scrap iron, doing so is absolutely useless. “

And the children, having one day away without permission, thinking that it had not use, picked up a pile of scrap iron piled outside the door of the blacksmith - I reported it immediately for that matter - Mr. Vilette thought that it was good to add:

- “Collecting scrap is encouraging the children to steal, and you can tell that to the Minister: I do not want my son to steal, I have a well-raised daughter - she went to Free School of Rabestiens - and I do not want my son to be a thief!”

So, because I obey my superiors, because I do my duty, I corrupt the youth by inciting them to theft! I do not need to say what a slap in the face these unspeakable and unexpected words were for me, condemning the role of the school and impugning my honor, delivered in public and peremptorily. Choking, I was left without words.

It is unfortunate to see that while some give their lives for the salvation of the country there are others behind the front lines, who, not satisfied with luxuriating the safety of their homes, hamper, with their unsuitable and injurious words, the coordinating efforts of those working for everybody’s protection and who conscientiously execute their given orders.

I thought that it was my duty to report such facts.

Sincerely, Primary School Inspector, my respectful greetings.

L. Mazens

Primary School Inspection for Albi West

Forwarded for action to the Prefect of Tarn. I would add that Mrs. Mazen’s husband is mobilized on the frontlines. Lisle-sur-Tarn March 6, 1940.

Managing Primary School Inspector

Forwarded to the Prefect. It seems desirable that Mr. Vilette be reminded, with a sound understanding, of his duties. Albi, March 8, 1940. To the Primary School Inspector, the Principal Officer,

NATIONAL POLICE

On this day 23 March 1940 at 10 o’clock.

We, the undersigned, SIGAL, Aimé and Mercadier, Maurice, gendarmes, at the residence of Lisle, Tarn department, wearing our uniform and under orders of our leaders, report that March 22, 1940, on patrol at the residence and acting pursuant to a request for information from the prefect of the Tarn, and dated March 13, 1940 (No. 1450 / 3 Section on the 16 of the same month) for the purpose of engaging in an investigation of objectionable speech made on the occasion of the salvage of old scrap by Mr. Villettes, Lapeyrière owner, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn (Tarn) have collected the following statement:

M. Serres, Marcel, 40, Inspector of Primary Education in Lisle s/ Tarn (Tarn), said:

“Around the 7 or 8 March 1940, Mrs. Mazen, a teacher at Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn sent me a letter in which she revealed to me that she had been subjected to vulgar insults hurled at her by Mr. Vilette inhabitant of Lapeyrière. He supposedly violently verbally assaulted Mrs. Mazen, in particular, and the administration, in general, concerning the instructions that were given to schools to carry out the collection of scrap metal by the students.

The terms of this letter and the language that she described seemed to me of such a nature that I did not hesitate to pass it to the appropriate authorities for action.”

Reading confirmed and signed.

According to this statement, seeing that this incident took place in Lapeyrière and continuing the investigation [we] have received the following statements:

1. - Mr. Galaup, Jules, 75, owner Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn (Tarn), said:

“On Sunday, September 7, 1940, around 3:00 p.m., I was in the hamlet of Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn (Tarn), when Mr. Vilette challenged Ms. Mazens, a teacher in said hamlet, about the salvage of scrap metal . Mr. Vilettes said: ‘I have a daughter who is honest and I do not want my son to be a thief, picking up scrap.’ Madame Mazens replied ‘I do what I was ordered.’ She seemed very touched by these words. On this I went home as did Mrs. Mazen.

I need to add that Mr. Vilettes did not insult Mrs. Mazen, he spoke politely.”

Reading confirmed and signed.

2. - Mrs. Robert, Juliette, born LARROQUE, age 29, housewife Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn, said:

“I was in the hamlet of Lapeyrière, Sunday, March 7, 1940, at 3:00 p.m., when Mr. Villettes, Norbert told Mrs. Mazen, the teacher in said place, that his son was not going to school to learn to be a thief and that from that day on he did not want him to go with his classmates to collect scrap metal. He added that he had a very honest daughter and he wanted his son to be the same. Ms. Mazens replied that his son would not return to the salvage of scrap metal.

In saying this Mr. Villettes was very polite, however I must say he seemed drunk.”

Reading confirmed and signed.

3. Mrs. Landes, Marie-Louise, born LAVAL, age 33, housewife in Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn, says

“On Sunday, March 7, around 3:00 p.m., I was in front of my house in Lapeyrière when Mr. Vilettes, challenged Ms. Mazens teacher at the said place in these terms: “I do not want my son to go collect scrap, I do not send him to school to learn to be a thief. I have an honest girl and I want my son to be the same.” Madame Mazens replied: “From this day your son will not participate in the salvage of scrap metal “and the incident was closed.

Mr. Vilettes spoke these words in a natural tone, without raising his voice, yet he seemed to have drunk more than usual.”

Reading confirmed and signed.

4. - Mr. Villettes, Norbert, 42, born June 6, 1897 in Lisle-sur-Tarn (Tarn) son of the late Elijah and Roucan Antoinette, property-owner in said place, the hamlet Lapeyrière, married with two children, can read and write, raised by his parents until the age of 21, neither decorated nor pensioner, class of 1917, temporary discharge no. 2, a French national, declared:

“It is true that on Sunday, March 7, 1940, around 3:00 p.m. while I was in the hamlet of Lapeyrière, I made a comment to Mrs. Mazen, a teacher at the said place, I told this person and this politely, “I do not want my son to enter uninhabited houses to collect scrap without being accompanied by the teacher.” And I added that I had a very honest girl and I wanted my son to be the same, meaning that I did not want my son to be a thief. I told her also that I did not want my son to go back to the collection of scrap metal and that for the reasons I stated above and because I knew that four students, including my son, had entered Mr. Binet’s house which is currently uninhabited.

Madame Mazens replied that from that day on my son would not collect scrap metal:

I certify that I spoke politely to Ms Mazens because I respect this person for whom I have nothing but praise. I do not remember uttering other words against the teacher. I do not have antipatriotic feelings and it was not for this purpose that I made this observation to Mrs. Mazen, because my son brought two bags of scrap iron that belonged to me when we started the salvage drive.

I regret having made such comments to Ms. Mazens since she took them the wrong way, in the future I will abstain.”

Reading confirmed and signed.

5. - Mr. SAISSAC, Paul, 42, mayor of the town of Lisle-sur-Tarn (Tarn) said:

“I have known for many years Mr. Vilettes, Norbert, residing in Lapeyrière, municipality of Lisle-sur-Tarn, he has a good reputation and is perfectly honest. He is absolutely incapable of knowingly making subversive comments about any subject whatsoever. Owner-farmer, he harvests a lot of white wine and perhaps loves a bit too much the product of his labor.”

Reading confirmed and signed.
