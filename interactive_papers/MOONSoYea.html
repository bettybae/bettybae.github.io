[Denise Domenach-Lallich], , “Une jeune fille libre: Journal” (May 10 - June 20, 1940)

Friday, May 10

Two important events:

1 - My grandfather, Papa's father, died in Hauterives last night.

2 - The Germans have bombed Lyon, Colmar, Lille, etc.

1 - Papa is very sad and so are we. We’re exhausted because it's been three nights that we can’t sleep because of the [air-raid] alerts. What shopping in the stores to get what we need for mourning [clothes], it’s tiring and it’s ruinously expensive.

2 - This evening, 5:20 to 6:45, we had an [air raid] alarm. We didn’t move our beds because we didn’t think there could be a real bombing. But the Boches [derogatory slang term for Germans] bombed Bron, the aerodrome located next door to us. We were lucky to not get anything, but in Bron there are about seventeen dead and I don’t know how many wounded. And most important the BOCHES invaded Belgium, Holland and Luxembourg. After Austria, Czechoslovakia, Poland, Denmark and Norway, this is starting to get ridiculous! I hope that the USA will finally decide to move. Apparently German planes have launched poisoned candy, each wrapped in a paper on which - oh bitter irony - are written these words: Peace Sweet.

Sunday, May 12

At the moment we have been having on [air raid] alert for an hour. I should be in a cellar. But I’m not afraid and I prefer to go about my usual activities rather than breathe the mold in a shelter. We have alerts all the time, two last night, one this morning. If we don’t take off to Hauterives, I’ll die of boredom. I really like listening to the bombs fall. I’m hearing the end of the alert ringing. It lasted one hour and forty-five minutes, we won’t have Mass… too late!

Yesterday, during the [air raid] alarm, with René, we went to see if we could see the German airplane and we saw it almost above our house; at that moment we heard a bang and a small white snowflake erupted just behind the airplane, it was the DCA [anti-aircraft], then it kept thundering for a quarter of an hour and shrapnel fell on a house next door to us. The Germans behave like barbarians: in Bron, they strafed the Poles next to their airplanes. In Poland, they were unbelievably savage. They tried to destroy all the Catholics and I read in the newspaper that they had killed one hundred and fifty scouts in Bydgoszcz.

Tuesday, May 14

With the death of my grandfather, we inherit the house in Hauterives, if the bombing continues, we will take refuge there. What luck!

Thursday, May 16, 10 o'clock in the evening

Yesterday was my birthday. As a present, I’ll be signed up for drawing lessons from the ABC school. Today, we still haven’t had an [air raid] alert. For six days we’ve had them all the time. This adds some distraction.

Friday, May 17, 2:30

My grandmother, Maman’s mother, is bugging me more and more; the other one, Papa's mother, was very nice and she told me lots of stories. Unfortunately, she is the one who died. Bonne-Maman told me that any idiot could pass their bachot [Baccalaureate]. In parentheses, she didn’t pass hers. She said that because she thinks I'll fail next year. She said it with a little air of superiority, which is absolutely infuriating, especially because she doesn’t even know Latin.

It’s 3:20 and we still haven’t had an [air raid] alarm today. Ah! that's it. I hear the sirens, but I still don’t hear the DCA [anti-aircraft fire]. I'd like it better if it happened while we were in class. We go down into the cellars with the teachers, some of them make us recite the rosary, others make us recite our Latin declensions, it’s a little less demoralizing, but still a drag. With Edith and Marie-Charlotte , we found a small cellar on the side where we hide to play cards. We like alerts because the time it takes to get out of the cellar, and the time it takes to get back to the classroom makes the morning go by.

May 24, 6:30

My legs are killing me because I went to the Palace of the Fair to receive the refugees. The scouts are allowed to miss school to go help refugees. It was in the newspaper. We begin by washing all the floor, it’s exhausting, then we dry with the mop, we spread sawdust, we set the tables for two hundred people, we bring in the refugees, we serve the refugees, we serve them all in two services, we do the dishes, we also eat, after that it’s finished and we leave. I really feel sorry for these poor refugees. Many children are separated from their parents and have no idea where they are. They fled from Belgium, Luxembourg and northern France in such haste and under fire from German machine guns who constantly harassed them. The French are charitable, it’s extraordinary, there are poor people who rob for the refugees, there are some who come every day to help. As for me, I'll go back almost every day.

Saturday June 1

This afternoon, we had three [air raid] alarms. The first lasted from noon to 2:15, but didn’t prevent us from having lunch. The second lasted from 2:30 to 3:30 and the third from 4 to 5:30 am. There are people who were unable to have lunch because they were always taken surprise outside by the successive alarms. Mom, who has just returned, told me that Boches bombed Givors, Saint-Rambert-d'Albon, etc… And there are already many dead. I think that tonight we’ll get our turn too. Maybe it is me who will soon receive a bomb on my rikiki [slang for head]. That would be really annoying to die so stupidly at only the age of fifteen. If I had to die, I would rather die bravely as a soldier and for a noble cause for which I would have, of my own will, sacrificed my life. Dying vulgarly, killed by a bomb and at the age of fifteen, just when I’m starting to enjoy life, to understand it, to love it, just when so many desires and goals fill me. Oh no! My God! Pity, not now, not yet. And yet if I could hasten the victory, absolutely, fine, it would be ok, but only if it [my death] was of some use.

"At the banquet of life, unfortunate invitee I appeared one day and I die I die and on my tomb where slowly I arrive No one will come to shed tears

Gilbert

Sunday, June 2

Tonight we had an alarm that lasted one hour. The DCA hit hard, but I didn’t hear any bombs falling. This morning there was one at 1:30am and we distinctly heard the bombs falling close to home. Bernard and I made fun of it, but Mireille and Mauritius were freaked out, Françoise kept making all of these absurd comments and Mom was in a fierce mood.

Tuesday, June 11, 8:20

Oh! Those Italian pigs! Those Macaroni bastards! They declared war [on us] yesterday evening. When we learned about it, we were in such a state of exasperation that we would have beaten up anyone… Groups of students formed up and they went in great pomp to tear down the flag of the Italian consulate; they burned it in the street. Some people even roughed up some Italians and ransacked shops; I saw them do it to the Banco di Roma. There were fights just about everywhere, even in my peaceful Saint-Maximin street.

Friday, June 14, 11:15

Things are bad, things are even very bad! Paul Reynaud sent out his last appeal to America, an SOS. The Germans are almost to Paris. As for the Macaronis, still nothing. I fear for France. "If the USA does not immediately declare war, extremely serious things will come to pass" said Paul Reynaud. And all of France was glued to the TSF [national radio network], well, at least those who have a radio set, because we don’t. Papa thinks that we make enough noise all by ourselves. All the schools will close on June 15 by an order of the Minister of National Education.

Thursday, June 20

What events since Friday! The Boches took Paris and came farther than Dole. So Sunday afternoon, at 3:00, Maman and Papa decided that we will also run away from the Boches. As for me, I was sick and I was constantly throwing up. Maman packed the trunks at full speed, she filled them with provisions and stuffed everything into the car, on the top of the car and on the racks of the bicycles, because we needed to leave like that: in the car, Maman and me in front, Bernard, Maurice, Françoise and Claude in the back. René left on the moped, Mireille and Madeleine, the maid, by bicycle. We had a rendez-vous at La Roche-de-Glun, at M. Decombe’s house, the godfather of Françoise, 100 kilometers south of Lyon. Monday morning, at 10:00am, Bon-Papa and Bonne-Maman arrived by car. What a cold shower! Then René, arrived at noon, Mireille and Madeleine at 1:00 in the afternoon. At 1:30 we got some information from the TSF and we listened to the speech of Marshall Pétain saying that defeated and invaded France had requested an armistice with the enemy. Bon-Papa ran out of the dining room like a madman, and from a distance, we could hear his sobs. It was impressive. It’s the sole proof of sensitivity that I’ve ever seen him show until now, that’s made me think better of him. All of us began to cry after hearing the death warrant of France and we were no longer able to swallow a single bite of our meal. Marshall Pétain said: “I have tried to cease combat.” Because of the static and our emotion, we didn’t hear very well, we understood: “I have ceased combat.” Maman decided, “It’s no longer worth it to go any further.” But in the evening, we listened to some information and when the speaker said, “violent battles are taking place near Lyon,” we understood our error and the next day, we left again: and I was in the car with my grandparents! We were all in the car and we had a very difficult trip stuck in heavy traffic until Pont-Saint Esprit where the gendarmes stopped us. Maman did a good job at arguing, she debated well because she wanted to go to Sainte-Maxime, to the villa of my grandparents. But the gendarmes said simply:"Nobody gets by." So we changed direction, Maman took the road back the other way, Bon-Papa followed her and we came to Cavil argues. It is where one of my aunts is from and we were well received, we slept where we could and the next day Maman rented a house. I forgot to say that during our trip from La Roche-Glun, we met a lot of trucks of troops going down to the south; on the narrow roads, there were lines of up to three cars. Of course, lots of accidents, for our part, we simply returned in a truck. I forgot to say that when we left Lyon, Papa had a terrible attack. And that, at La Roche, to take the cyclists in his car, Bon-Papa had to leave the trunks of silver, furs, and especially Tom, a dirty mutt he liked very much. Mom has rented an old café-tobacco shop, where we're not doing too badly. Obviously, we sleep two per bed and we do not have running water, but finally we can make do. What’s the most annoying is the toilets which are at the back of the garden and are simply a hole in the ground and two small steps to put your feet on. So, for me, it makes me constipated.

Sunday, June 23

Since we arrived it has been raining.

June 30, 9 o'clock in the morning

That's it. The armistice is signed. France has capitulated. When we found out about it, two or three days ago, we were decimated, we couldn’t believe it.

From Denise Domenech-Lallich, Une jeune fille libre: Journal (1939-1944) (Paris: Arènes, 2005), English translation by Chad Denton.
