— “The greatest industrial organization in the history of the world” —


At the beginning of October 1915, the round advertising posts that studded Berlin’s streets displayed the latest declaration of the Prussian War Ministry’s War Raw Materials Department, the Kriegsrohstoffabteilung. In bold Gothic script, the red posters explained that the period for the voluntary delivery of requisitioned “finished, used, and unused objects made of copper, brass, and pure nickel” had been extended for an additional two weeks.[1]

Twenty-five-year old Mary Ethel McAuley, an art student who also wrote as a freelancer for the Pittsburgh Dispatch, had just arrived from the United States and she was surprised to see the collection centers set up in vacant storefronts throughout the city.[2] When McAuley began speaking to German women, somebody showed her the list of objects to be requisitioned that all households had received. As McAuley later recalled,
It was a long list, but the main thing on it was the brass oven doors. As nearly every room in Germany has a stove [for heating] with two of these doors about a foot wide and three quarters of a foot high you can get some idea of how much material this collection brought. Since this collection the doors have been replaced with iron ones that are not nearly so pretty. All kinds of brass pots and kettles were collected, but with special permits people were allowed to keep their heirlooms. Everything was paid for by the weight, artistic value counted for naught.[3]
The notice that McAuley saw also listed cooking kettles, baking tins, frying pans, sauce pans, bowls, platters, hot water tanks, and water heaters, with exceptions made for bathtubs, light fixtures, smoking sets, and tins used to store food. McAuley failed to mention that anybody who did not report their metal could be imprisoned for up to six months or face a 10,000 Mark fine.[4]

During her two years studying art in Germany, McAuley noted the gradual spread of these increasingly harsh measures. She thought that the first copper collection had provided the Germans “enough metal to last several years,” but if needed, the government could eventually take “the brass door knobs which are very large and heavy.” She witnessed the removal of copper roofs from public buildings and heard about churches sacrificing their bells. By the spring of 1917, McAuley explained, households—already stripped of most of their copper, brass, and nickel possessions—had to declare all their aluminum objects.[5] The official decree listed 357 items including ashtrays, cocktail glasses, omelet pans, pudding molds, and toothpaste tubes.[6] Just before McAuley left Berlin in July 1917, the brass door knobs were added to the list.[7]

When McAuley returned to the United States and published her observations in Germany in War Time: What An American Girl Saw and Heard, she argued that these requisitions partly explained how the German people had effectively countered Allied economic warfare:
When the blockade of Germany began, no one believed that she could hold out without supplies from the outside world; that in a short time her people would be starving and that she would be out of raw material. During the few months before the blockade was declared, Germany had shipped into her ports as much cotton, copper, rubber, and food as was possible. After the blockade started much stuff was obtained from Holland and Scandinavia. From the very first days of the war Germany set to work to utilize all the material that she had on hand, and her watchword to her people was ‘waste nothing.’[8]
For McCauley, this success came less from government initiative, and more from the values of the German people—what she called “German Sparsamkeit” or thriftiness, combined with a unique ability to obey rules. She saw these metal requisitions of valuable household objects as an innovative and necessary response to material deprivation, akin to the contemporaneous collections of waste products, such as used paper, scrap rubber, and even cherry pits. Ignoring the coercive measures to force compliance, McAuley understood all of these activities as collective, voluntary actions, telling her American readers, “one has no idea of the tremendous sacrifices these people are making for their country.”[9]

McAuley’s sympathetic portrait of the German people at war clashed with the depiction of German barbarism disseminated by American propaganda.[10] Even before the United States declared war on Germany in April 1917, the American press cited the forced requisitions of Belgian, French, Polish, and Russian metal as examples of German “Huns” despoiling occupied Europe, willfully violating international law.[11] Mentions of domestic German requisitions served to illustrate German desperation and weakness, not strength.[12] Not surprisingly, US government censors immediately banned McAuley’s book for its “pro-German” stance.[13] At the same time, however, some Americans openly admitted their admiration for German industrial organization, seeing it, like McAuley, as a clever, organized, and efficient response to total war. An article by the Berlin correspondent of the Chicago Daily News at the end of August 1915 described the German department responsible for the metal requisitions as “the greatest industrial organization in the history of the world.”[14] When McAuley’s book was finally released to the public in 1918, one reviewer said that it provided an answer “to those among us of Germany’s enemies who occasionally pause in our wholesale condemnations of her to marvel how she can endure so long.”[15] Another reviewer took the argument one step further, claiming that “the lesson for us is obvious. If Americans are going to beat the enemy, they must learn how he does things and then do them even better.”[16]

During the war and in its immediate aftermath, countless studies of the German war economy attempted to answer the question of how the Germans managed to last so long and their answers mirrored the American ambivalence. Economists, engineers, and technicians focused on the domestic activities of the KRA, describing the production of previously untapped resources, the technological development of substitute products, and the complicated system of price controls, mandatory declarations, and sequestrations of raw materials.[17] Legal experts and diplomats described the widespread, methodical pillage of occupied Europe by the Germans and their Austro-Hungarian allies.[18] It was the rare observer, such as the French economist Henri Hauser, who could take a position between “disdainful ignorance” and “unreserved admiration” and carefully evaluate what other governments should, and should not, “imitate” of German practices.[19]

In the interwar period, the most comprehensive effort to evaluate German economic organization and occupation policy was contained within a series of 152 volumes published by the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace between 1921 and 1937. The series systematically documented the social and economic costs of the First World War on all fronts, including the German and Austro-Hungarian occupation zones in Belgium, France, Serbia, Romania, Italy, Poland, and Russia.[20] Though the project as a whole integrated the home front and front line experience of the conflict from a transnational perspective (a “scholarly equivalent to the League of Nations”), the individual volumes—often written by wartime administrators and cabinet members—reflected national experiences, and thus perpetuated the vision of German wartime requisitions as either ruthless expropriation or admirable sacrifice.[21]

Interest in the German war economy from 1914 to 1918 then faded in the wake of the Second World War, when that mixture of repulsion and admiration shifted to the Nazi economic planners; the Carnegie volumes on the war economy of both Germany and Austria-Hungary are thus still among the best works on the topic.[22] The transnational approach of the Carnegie project was lost as well, as subsequent studies of both world wars remained confined within national borders (primarily those of Germany, France, and Great Britain) and neglected the experience of occupied Europe.[23] But that has begun to change. In the past fifteen years, historians have turned their attention to the occupied zones of the First World War on both fronts, and have taken a closer look at the claims made by the victims of German aggression. The killing of more than 6400 Belgian and French civilians in the 1914 German invasion, previously believed by most historians to be fabrications of Allied propaganda, has now been verified, opening up the door to a reexamination of German behavior in all occupied zones.[24] What caused the systematic plunder, deportations, and devastation in German-occupied Belgium, northern France, and eastern Europe? Explanations have focused on what Carnegie series editor James Shotwell described in 1940 as the “German theory of war,” recast as either a European-wide “dynamic of destruction” rooted not in military strategy but in “the mentalities, cultural assumptions, and fears of the troops” or—as in the most influential of these interpretations—on an “institutional extremism” within German military culture that pushed German military planners to abandon international law and treat occupied civilians “as objects of military necessity.”[25] The most recent work has addressed this question by comparing German practices with those of Austro-Hungarian troops in occupied Serbia, Poland, Romania and Ukraine.[26]

This work on the systematic economic exploitation of civilians in occupation zones remains surprisingly disconnected from the experience of German and Austro-Hungarian civilians.[27] The metal requisitions that McAuley witnessed in Germany—as other observers at the time noted—were nearly identical to those being carried out simultaneously in Austria-Hungary and all their occupied territories.[28] The key driver of these policies was the Prussian War Ministry’s War Raw Materials Department, the Kriegsrohstoffabteilung or KRA. Under the civilian leadership of industrialist Walther Rathenau, the KRA instituted widespread, methodical requisitions from businesses and industries in the newly occupied territories beginning in August 1914. These requisitions did not initially include goods in private households. After Rathenau’s resignation and replacement with a Prussian officer in April 1915, the KRA began systematic requisitions from German civilians, then subsequently extended these policies to the occupied territories. The KRA also persuaded the Austro-Hungarians to institute similar programs. By the end of the war, the combined German and Austro-Hungarian effort comprised “millions of individual cases of seizure,” from oven doors to church bells.[29]

Historians have downplayed the “rational ruthlessness” of the KRA, in part, because they have focused on the KRA’s unlikely origin story: explaining how “a Jewish businessman [Rathenau] stormed the ramparts of the apparently conservative and anti-Semitic Prussian army.”[30] The attention given to Rathenau’s role has minimized not only the increasing militarization of the KRA but also, paradoxically, the importance of German Jewish industrialists, particularly those involved in the trade and salvage of metal.[31] Jews, historically associated with the metal trade, had a virtual monopoly on the scrap metal business during the First World War, from the village scrap dealer to the wholesale broker. In 1913, 70% of Germany’s non-ferrous metal trading firms were Jewish and the KRA tapped the leaders of these firms to manage Germany’s metal supply.[32] If the KRA, in the words of Gerald Feldman “saved the German war effort from disaster,” it was due, in part, to German Jewish expertise.[33] Without understanding the crucial role of German Jews in the requisition and salvage of raw materials in the First World War, it is not possible to appreciate fully the genesis of Nazi requisition and salvage policies in Germany in the 1930s and in occupied Europe in the 1940s.

Similarly, economic relations between Germany and Austria-Hungary prefigured how Nazi German authorities would later use economic constraints to force the governments of occupied societies to do their bidding. On July 31, 1914, Germany’s ban on exports and limitations on transit of goods provoked severe metal shortages in Austria-Hungary, leaving the empire dependent on German imports. In the case of copper, the KRA used this dependency as leverage. For each German delivery of copper or brass, the KRA required Austria-Hungary to provide half the weight in scrap. When the Austrians failed to meet those quotas, they passed legislation for domestic requisitions and confiscations modeled on Germany.[34] A close look at the timing and language of this Austro-Hungarian legislation reveals that each ostensibly independent program—whether industrial requisitions or voluntary donations—followed similar efforts in Germany.[35] The establishment of metal collections by Nazi Germany’s allies in the Second World War echoed this Austro-Hungarian experience in the First World War.

Though the central KRA authorities in Berlin intended their policies to be executed uniformly throughout Europe, in practice, implementation varied widely. In some cases, those charged with carrying out the requisitions pushed back against the measures in order to manage public opinion. Starting in 1917, public officials within Germany began calling for a halt to domestic requisitions and more systematic exploitation of the occupied territories. At the same time, the governor-general in Poland decreased the punitive measures for non-compliance and increased economic incentives to acquire the metal. But in other cases, military commanders or individual troops used the legislation as a pretext for outright theft and expropriation of private property.[36] Similar struggles would characterize Nazi German economic exploitation of Europe in the Second World War.

The study of these requisitions offers, in microcosm, a way to connect the larger global forces of economic warfare to both Germany’s occupation practices and the European-wide local struggles of daily life. Military necessity—fed by economic imperatives—led to the systematic economic exploitation of civilians under German and Austro-Hungarian control. That exploitation fed resentment, which, to borrow Shotwell’s words, sowed “dragons’ teeth . . . for a harvest of hatred and non-cooperation on the part of the sufferers.”[37]

When German troops invaded Belgium on August 4, 1914, Germany had no organization dedicated to economic mobilization. This lack of foresight used to be attributed to the widespread, and illusory, conviction that it would be a short war.[38] Recent research, however, has suggested that though nearly all the soldiers who left for the front in August 1914 expected to be home by Christmas, many of those who sent them knew that the war might last much longer.[39] In the early 1900s German military planners began considering the economic implications of a long war (one to two years) carried out under a British naval blockade.[40] These questions took on new urgency in 1911 when a dispute between German and French iron mining interests in western Morocco fanned the flames of economic nationalism and threatened to provoke a full-scale war between the Germans, French, and British.[41] German economists, members of special interest groups who advocated for military expansion, and powerful industrialists who depended on overseas raw materials repeatedly petitioned German political and military authorities to prepare for a future economic war. Though none of their proposals initially succeeded, they laid the groundwork for the establishment of the KRA on August 13, 1914, which rapidly and radically transformed German economic organization over the next four years.

On May 11, 1912, members of the newly-founded German Army League gathered in their clubhouse in Berlin to hear a speech by the Lübeck iron magnate Emil Ludwig Possehl.[42] After introducing himself as both a “Hanseatic businessman” and a veteran of the Franco-Prussian War, Possehl explained to his audience that the greatest danger facing Germany—what could “force us to our knees,” he said—was not a land war with France, but, rather, an economic war with England. Possehl then outlined Germany’s extreme dependence on overseas trade and raised the specter of a British blockade “from the Low-Countries to the Baltic Sea” that could immediately paralyze German industry. His statistics painted a picture of economic Armageddon: financial collapse, mass unemployment, and starvation. To avoid this grim future, Possehl called for the creation of an “economic General Staff” led by the “best men from industry, trade, and finance as well as agriculture.”[43] After his talk, Possehl carefully prepared 200 copies of his speech and distributed them secretly to trusted friends who shared his fears, encouraging them to pass the speech to others. Shortly thereafter Possehl met with the Secretary of State for the Interior Clemens von Delbrück, the minister responsible for Germany’s economy, and explained his idea.[44] By the following summer, as Possehl confided to the German economist Arthur Dix, his speech had reached “the highest offices in the Reich, in the army, and in the navy” and had, apparently, been received with “extraordinary agreement.”[45]

Two months after Possehl’s Berlin speech, Walther Rathenau approached Germany’s chancellor Theobald von Bethmann Hollweg with similar concerns.[46] Rathenau shared Possehl’s worries about Germany’s overseas dependence on iron ore and other raw materials. Indeed, two years earlier Rathenau had attempted to mediate the competing Moroccan iron ore claims of the French consortium Union des Mines and the upstart German company Mannesmann Brothers.[47] As he explained to Bethmann Hollweg, and later expressed in an essay published in a Viennese newspaper, Rathenau saw the 1911 Morocco crisis that grew out of that dispute as symptomatic of a larger problem.[48] He thought that colonial territories and spheres of influence were necessary, not for their prestige or for their markets, but rather, for the raw materials they contained in their soil; “ore deposits,” Rathenau predicted, “will one day be more valuable than the armored cruisers that are forged from their veins.” But when it came to colonies, he thought, Germany had “missed the era of major acquisitions,” leaving German trade vulnerable to foreign legislation, tariffs, economic controls, and other unfair competition. Though Rathenau openly admitted that Germany needed more land and should have a more “equitable share” in the future, he did not advocate military conquest. Nor did he propose the opposite—abandoning any aspiration for great power status. Instead, he had an economic solution. Rathenau proposed the creation of a central European customs union whose economic dominance would necessarily draw in the western powers, rivaling the American economic sphere. Rathenau hoped that this peaceful development would “rob nationalist hatred among the nations of its sharpest sting,” and he predicted optimistically that “if Europe’s economy melts into one common unity . . . so will its politics.”[49] Up to August 1914, Rathenau continued to press this idea in his conversations with his regular house guest and neighbor in the Prussian countryside, Bethmann Hollweg.[50]

Besides Possehl and Rathenau, the greatest advocate of an “economic General Staff” and a central European customs union was the economist Arthur Dix. He published widely on the topic and sent copies of his articles to Bethmann Hollweg, Helmuth von Moltke (Chief of the General Staff), Alfred von Tirpitz (Chief of the Admiralty Staff), and several other prominent political and military leaders.[51] The weight of their combined appeals seemed to pay off in November 1912 when Bethmann Hollweg convinced the skeptical Delbrück to head up a standing committee on economic mobilization.[52]

Though notions of a controlled economy were anathema to his economic liberalism, Delbrück reluctantly began to assess the scale of the problem. In 1905 the Imperial Naval Office had determined that of 440 companies they surveyed in the Ruhr industrial region, most had enough stocks of raw materials to last three months, only 20 percent could maintain production for up to eight months, and just 2 percent could last more than a year.[53] When Delbrück’s Interior Ministry led a similar survey of the mining industry in 1913 the situation had changed little, with most companies reporting between two and three months of stocks.[54] The official trade statistics for that year showed that raw materials comprised over 57% of all German imports; a British naval blockade could clearly strangle Germany’s economic potential.[55] In their depictions of the dangers faced by Germany, both Possehl and Rathenau had focused on the iron and steel industry, which relied on 12 million tons of imported ore from Sweden, Spain, France, the Mediterranean and Russia, roughly 1/3 of the industry’s needs.[56] But the situation for non-ferrous metals was even more serious.[57] In 1913, Germany consumed 26% of the world’s copper (259,300 tons), 17% of the world’s nickel (5000 tons), and 15% of the world’s tin (19,300 tons), most of which was imported by ship: from the United States, Chile, Bolivia, Canada, British Malay and the French colony of New Caledonia.[58] Despite increased imports of manganese ore, nickel, tin, brass, and tungsten in late 1913 and early 1914, as well as significant stockpiles held by some private companies (like the Krupp armaments manufacturer), Germany remained dependent on foreign imports for most strategic minerals.[59]

The proposed economic reforms, however, quickly became mired in bureaucratic and political disputes.[60] Bethmann Hollweg reportedly waited months before meeting Possehl about his ideas for a centrally organized economy, and once he did, “members of the bureaucratic hierarchy” blocked Possehl’s access to the Kaiser. “After much back and forth talk,” Bethmann Hollweg passed Possehl’s proposal to the economic mobilization committee. When that committee met on May 26, 1914, Possehl, along with several other representatives from industry, repeated his proposal for an “economic General Staff,” but the committee decided to table the issue until their next meeting. That meeting fell on July 31, 1914, the first day of the German mobilization and never took place.[61] Efforts for a central European customs union faced similar temporizing. Despite the pressure of outside interest groups like the Mitteleuropäischer Wirtschaftsverein or the Deutsch-Österreichisch-Ungarische Wirtschaftsverband (founded in the fall of 1913), German tariff policy remained unchanged at the start of the war.[62]

Germany’s political leaders were unaware of the doubts harbored by their military leadership about the country’s chances of a quick victory, so most (including Bethmann Hollweg) assumed that a future war would be short, and, thus, did not appreciate the urgent need for preparations until far too late.[63] This was not the case, however, for those in the highest military circles. When Possehl and Rathenau initially expressed their worries to Delbrück and Bethmann-Hollweg in the summer of 1912, Moltke already had serious doubts about Germany’s ability either to win a short war or to endure a long one.[64] Following the outbreak of the Balkan Wars in October 1912, between the Russia-sponsored Balkan League and Turkey, Moltke had to accept the possibility of a future war on two fronts. “We must prepare ourselves,” he wrote to the Prussian War Ministry on November 1, “for a protracted campaign with a great many severe, prolonged battles, until we force down one of our enemies; the exertion and the consumption of resources must increase, if we have to achieve victory in different theaters of war in the west and east successively.”[65] Moltke was particularly concerned about the scale of resources that would be required. In 1910, Moltke had read Arthur Dix’s article “Germany’s Economic Future in War and Peace,” which laid out many of the ideas later proposed by Possehl and Rathenau. In a letter to the author, Moltke assured Dix that German authorities understood the problem of the raw material supply and had secretly begun preparatory measures, but these measures were “by no means complete.”[66] After 1913, Moltke’s General Staff was represented on the economic mobilization committee, and he could observe first-hand how ineffective these measures actually were.[67]

Why, then, did Moltke not do more to prepare? The War Ministry had managed to increase some imports of strategic raw materials beginning in 1913. He may also, as has been suggested, have felt that even though the industrial surveys pointed to shortages in many sectors, Germany had sufficient access to the most important resources for waging war—iron and coal. Furthermore, those estimates of low reserves did not take into account that raw materials used in exports would be redirected for domestic consumption in wartime.[68] But even had his information been perfect, there was relatively little Moltke could do. He had no decision-making power over matters of the domestic economy, and there was no “economic General Staff.” The economy was strictly the purview of the Interior Minister. On May 14, 1914, Moltke pushed Delbrück to take more aggressive preparatory measures, telling him, “a perhaps lengthy war on two fronts can only be sustained by an economically strong people.”[69] These words had little impact. Delbrück’s committee failed to take any decisive action.

But in one crucial area where Moltke had supreme decision-making authority—the drafting of the invasion plan—he made two significant decisions that had positive long-term economic impacts for Germany’s war economy.[70] First, in 1908, he modified the invasion plan of his predecessor, Alfred von Schlieffen, to shift more troops to Alsace-Lorraine. Moltke’s deputy Erich Ludendorff later claimed that the change served partly to prevent the French from occupying the Saar region, with its valuable coal fields and low-grade iron mines essential for German steel production.[71] Second, in 1911, Moltke rerouted the planned invasion again to avoid passing through southern Holland, thus maintaining Dutch neutrality. This decision rested primarily on his projections of the economic consequences of a long war.[72] As he explained in a memorandum at the time,
A hostile Holland at our back could have disastrous consequences for the advance of the German army to the west, particularly if England should use the violation of Belgian neutrality as a pretext for entering the war against us. A neutral Holland secures our rear, because if England declares war on us for violating Belgian neutrality she cannot herself violate Dutch neutrality. She cannot break the very law whose sake she goes to war. Furthermore it will be very important to have in Holland a country whose neutrality allows us to have imports and supplies. She must be the windpipe that enables us to breathe.[73]
Moltke accurately foresaw the extent—and limitations—of the future naval blockade, understanding that if Germany and Austria-Hungary were to go to war against France, Britain, and Russia, “not only the German coastlines, but also most of the Austrian and Italian coasts, will be blockaded.”[74] Whereas Possehl, in his 1912 lecture, imagined a blockade from the “Low-Countries to the Baltics” and assumed that the British would not respect Dutch neutrality, Moltke had a better grasp of the importance of international law for British war aims.[75] Yet his interpretation of this neutrality may have been too generous and overly broad. He assumed that raw material imports “under the American flag” or from other neutrals could transit unimpeded to Germany.[76] Here, Moltke may have been influenced by Arthur Dix, who also believed that Dutch neutrality guaranteed access to imports such as American copper.[77]

An entirely different response to the raw material crisis was proposed by Norbert Levy, the owner of a small copper trading firm in Berlin and the founder and director of the Association of German Metal Traders.[78] On February 12, 1913, Levy gave a public lecture that sought to reassure those who worried that Germany would be unable to meet its growing need for copper in the near future, particularly in the case of a war.[79] He argued that Germany had a heretofore largely untapped reserve: the market in scrap metal. Using the example of copper (his specialty), Levy revealed the inner-workings of this extensive, and little noticed market. In small towns or villages, itinerant scrap metal dealers, often using a hand- or dog-pulled cart, collected everyday metal objects, such as broken or used doorknobs, window latches, lamps, and clock pendulums. They sold them to scrap metal brokers (such as Levy), who identified, sorted, and weighed the objects and then resold them in bulk to foundries and refineries who transformed the metal into bars, wires, or plates. The same process worked at an industrial scale—though the objects included used boilers from steam engines, distilleries from breweries, copper wire from telephone, telegraph, or street-car lines, and even spent munitions from abandoned battlefields. Indeed, as Levy noted, recent wars had greatly benefited the world’s scrap copper supply; Manchuria had exported considerable amounts of used artillery shells following the Russo-Japanese War and Japan had acquired over 6000 tons of demonetized copper coins following their subsequent annexation of Korea in 1905. Levy predicted that similar quantities would be available “in the near future” from the Balkans—an understatement that failed to foresee the geographical extent and exponentially greater munitions needs of the First World War.[80]

Despite accurately anticipating the importance of scrap metal for the German war economy, Levy’s analysis suffered from one significant shortcoming: his assumption that market forces would remain unchanged during the war. The outbreak of war on August 4, 1914, and the announcement of the British blockade a day later threatened to cut off the overseas metal supply, causing a sudden rise in demand. Those who had metal reserves had an incentive to hoard and sell at a higher price, instantly creating bottlenecks for both civilian and military production. At the same time, the mobilization of soldiers called many scrap dealers, metal brokers, refiners, engineers, and miners to the front lines, leaving behind few with enough knowledge and expertise to maintain the infrastructure necessary to keep German factories supplied with metal.[81]

This was the situation faced by the leadership of Germany’s largest consumer of copper, the Allgemeine Elektrizitäts Gesellschaft (AEG) in the first week of August. As head of the technical-economic section of the Metals Division, the 33-year-old engineer Wichard von Moellendorff had to determine how to keep AEG’s numerous plants provisioned with copper, lead, and zinc.[82] He estimated Germany’s copper reserves to be just 20,000 tons, only 8% of Germany’s annual peacetime consumption. Domestic production of an inferior grade of copper brought in annually only an additional 25,000 tons. Lead posed a “still greater dilemma.” The situation was particularly worrisome because AEG alone accounted for 1/7 of Germany’s annual copper and lead consumption.[83] His superior, Heinrich Peierls, contacted the War Department about the Belgian zinc supply, while Moellendorff decided to draft a more ambitious proposal.[84] As a devotee of the American efficiency expert, Frederick Taylor, Moellendorff concluded that the only way to manage a situation with an increasingly constrained supply and rising demand was to create a central allocation system under military authority. As a young junior manager, Moellendorff did not have enough clout to convince the War Department, but he knew somebody who did. On August 8, Moellendorff sent his proposal to his friend and mentor at AEG, the company president Walther Rathenau.[85]

After August 4, Rathenau had begun asking his friends in the Reichstag about Germany’s preparations for requisitioning and allocating raw materials, particularly those from newly invaded Belgian territory. Through these contacts, he arranged a meeting on August 8 with Colonel Heinrich Scheüch, the head of the War Department.[86] That same day Rathenau received Moellendorff’s proposal. Though Rathenau thought the idea of a centralized system unnecessary, he he mentioned it to Scheüch, who sent him the very next day to the War Minister, Erich von Falkenhayn.[87] There, Rathenau confronted Falkenhayn about his abysmal preparations, telling him:
Your Excellency, you have prepared the military mobilization as meticulously and carefully as possible. You have lists for every man, every horse, every gun and their destinations. Have you also similarly prepared the economic mobilization? The war may last a long time [and] what will you do if the raw materials that are essential for warfare—such as zinc, copper, aluminum, etc.—run out? Do you also have lists for those?[88]
Because the war could last “several months” and industry needed access to raw materials, Rathenau insisted that an efficient organization be put in place to manage these materials in all German-controlled territory. This organization could create a list of firms, determine the raw material requirements, and allocate their use in order to avoid “a sudden shortage of scarce raw materials” and increase “the production of substitute products as well as war goods in general.”[89] Given his previous experience with Prussian officialdom, Rathenau had little hope that his proposal would succeed. Moreover, when it came to requisitions, the War Ministry took the position that “you can’t apportion out the Belgian bear before he’s been killed.”[90] To Rathenau’s surprise, just a week later on August 13, 1914, Falkenhayn created the Kriegsrohstoffabteilung with the mission to do exactly what Rathenau had requested: namely, to manage all domestic raw materials as well as those found in the future, “upon successful progress of the army in the occupied enemy territories, and possibly in friendly Austria.”[91] At its head would be Rathenau and a Prussian military officer, Karl Oehme—appointed to meet the objections of Prussian military leaders who did not want to see such an important war office under the direction of a civilian and a Jew.[92]

As German armies moved through northern France and countered the Russian attack in East Prussia, the KRA’s first five employees commandeered four small offices in the War Ministry. Besides Oehme, they all initially came from AEG: Georg Klingenberg, Moellendorff, Rathenau, and Rathenau’s private secretary.[93] On August 14, the second day of operations, Heinrich Peierls gave a preliminary report on German metal stocks, elaborating on Moellendorff’s earlier rough estimates: German domestic production of lead met 35% of demand, aluminum 9%, copper 8% while German production of metals like tin, nickel, tungsten, or chromium was “virtually nil.”[94] With very little time, they decided to extrapolate the amount of stockpiled raw materials in the country by surveying the hundreds of companies that had supply contracts with the War Ministry. Working fifteen-hour days, Rathenau’s team determined three weeks later that reserves would run out in five months.[95]

Initially the most pressing concern was non-ferrous metal. Despite Possehl’s earlier fears of a collapse in the steel industry, the takeover of iron mines in Belgium, Luxembourg, and northeastern France as well as continued imports from Sweden more than made up for any iron blocked by the British.[96] The manufacture of high quality steels, however, required hardening agents such as manganese, tungsten, and nickel. Alloyed with steel, nickel produced powerful armor plate resistant to the new, armor-piercing explosive shells. By 1914, the German navy had adopted an armor plate with 3.73% nickel, and Germany’s leading armaments manufacturer, Krupp, had adapted nickel-steel for use in gun barrels and shell casings.[97] During the war they perfected its use in torpedoes, vehicle parts, and armored vehicles. The war would eventually consume 90% of the world’s nickel supply, including an estimated 1200 tons of nickel used in the cooking pots of field mess kitchens.[98] Krupp had secured two years of nickel reserves through additional imports before the war, meaning that the most immediate need was for copper and its alloys.[99] German cartridge cases for bullets and shells used brass—an alloy of 72% copper and 28% zinc. In its pure form tin served as solder to fuse other metals and as a coating for iron and steel cans to preserve food, but alloyed with copper, tin made bronze, whose anti-corrosion properties made it ideal for fuses, percussion caps, naval equipment, torpedo firing tubes, and propellers.[100]

To meet the demand for metals, Rathenau tackled the problem both internally within the KRA’s administrative structure and externally through a partnership with private industry. He began by assigning Moellendorff to chemicals and Klingenberg to textiles and metals, then—as they brought in more employees and moved to new offices—he set up a central section for metals (Section M), split into sub-sections for foreign affairs, mines, and requisitions. The foreign affairs office (Ma) regulated all imports and exports from allies, neutrals, and occupied territories, and supervised raw materials offices and foreign representatives abroad; the mines office (Mb) worked to extend domestic mineral production, controlled the mineral production of occupied territories, and prospected for new deposits. The requisitions office (Mc) carried out the surveys and statistics of stocks, determined need, and allocated or sequestered metal.[101] On August 26, without apparently consulting the Interior Secretary or the Prussian Ministry of Trade and Commerce, Rathenau met with Norbert Levy and several other representatives from Germany’s most important metal trading firms, foundries, and refineries to discuss the formation of a “war metals company.” He overcame their resistance by using AEG’s ability to offer favorable contracts and allocations of raw materials to those who participated.[102] After several days of meetings, the industrialists formed a supervisory board, determined the financing, and founded the Kriegsmetall AG on September 2, 1914. This was the first of Rathenau’s “war raw materials corporations” [Kriegsrohstoffgesellschaften], private stock companies supervised by the KRA and tasked with acquiring raw materials. The KRA created twelve more by early 1915, and, by the end of the war, would supervise nearly 200 of these organizations.[103]

From the beginning, the activities of Kriegsmetall AG were dominated by the two firms most concerned in the non-ferrous metal trade—AEG and the Metallgesellschaft AG (Germany’s largest metal trading firm). Rathenau and Max von der Porten (general director of a subsidiary of the Metallgesellschaft) served as state commissioners with veto power over all decisions of the fourteen-member supervisory board.[104] That board included the directors of the Metallgesellschaft and AEG, two directors of firms with close ties to AEG, as well as other large trading firms such as those run by Norbert Levy and Aron Hirsch.[105] The strong representation of the metal trading firms also meant that much of the decision-making was controlled by men of Jewish background. More than half of the board members were Jewish, including all of the representatives from the metal trade.[106] This predominance reflected the place of Jews in that industry, not only in Germany, but also elsewhere in Europe, Great Britain, and particularly the United States.[107] Germany’s relative mineral poverty made it difficult for German Jewish metal trading firms to own extensive mine holdings as did the Guggenheims in the United States and the Monds in Canada.[108] But these firms—Levy & Co, Hirsch & Sons, the Metallgesellschaft, Beer, Sondheimer & Co—relied on similar transnational family and business networks that gave them a global presence.[109] Before the war, Norbert Levy had traveled to Canada looking to secure supplies of nickel and copper from near Lake Superior.[110] On a later trip to the United States in June 1914, he gave a speech in New York to the Metal Division of the American Society for Waste Trades, where he spoke about his organization of Berlin metal traders. The Americans made him an honorary member of their association and Levy invited them to visit Berlin.[111] Even as the war cut off those transnational ties, that knowledge of overseas markets and those business relationships proved invaluable for the KRA’s attempts to acquire metal through imports from neutral countries.

With the establishment of the Kriegsmetall AG, Norbert Levy had accomplished his pre-war plan of putting the scrap industry in the service of any future war effort. Despite a clear conflict of interest, Levy maintained his position as the head of Berlin’s metal trading association, and as a board member of the Kriegsmetall AG made decisions that affected over 30,000 German companies.[112] If Levy had achieved his goals by September 1914, then so had Possehl. The “economic General Staff” that Possehl first proposed in his 1912 speech was now embodied, to a greater or lesser extent, in Rathenau’s organization. “Such an authoritative body,” Possehl had said then, “might be efficiently useful, and, in case of war, would prevent us from committing economic blunders of all kinds.”[113] Rathenau overcame the pre-war political sclerosis that had hobbled economic preparations by drawing on a team of engineers, men ready—in the words of the KRA Metal Section chief—“to plan, to construct, and to build.”[114]

Their ethos perfectly fit the cautious optimism of August 1914, when most still hoped that the war could be over by Christmas. But just three days after the founding of the Kriegsmetall AG, from September 6 to 9, the French countered the German attack at the Battle of the Marne, forcing a race to the English Channel that resulted in a series of fortified trenches from Basel to Dunkirk. Moltke suffered a nervous breakdown and Falkenhayn took de facto command. The ambitious planning in those first few weeks allowed the German war economy to continue to function for the duration of the long war that followed. With the beginning of the war of attrition, where defensive capabilities trumped offensive and Germany became a blockaded fortress, the activities of the KRA entered a new stage—one dominated by Rathenau’s idea of a central European economic union, retooled to the reality of German military supremacy.

— “Robbery as a Science” —


Soon after Rathenau stepped down as head of the KRA in April 1915, he sat down for an interview with Raymond Swing, the Berlin correspondent of the Chicago Daily News.[115] The American wanted to understand how Germany had managed to defy the British blockade and maintain economic production for eight months of war. For his part, Rathenau probably saw the interview as an opportunity both to counter Allied propaganda and to cement his legacy as the “economic general” who single-handedly saved the German war effort. Rathenau told Swing why he originally approached Falkenhayn in August 1914, explaining his hope that,
Germany could be remade into a self-supporting State, independent of imports. And if this could be done, then Germany, the blockaded, would end the war without a foreign debt while Great Britain, the blockader, would constantly be buying from foreign countries and paying the equivalent of cash for much of what she bought. So that at the end of the war the most extraordinary paradox conceivable would result; the blockader would be poor, the blockaded financially strong.[116]
It is tempting to read Rathenau’s statement as rhetorical “bluff,” a bombastic claim meant for an Allied audience; Swing’s own enthusiastic characterization of this “plan” as “one of the greatest ideas of modern times” heightens that perception.[117] But behind its obvious propagandistic purpose, Rathenau’s claim reveals a clear continuity in his thought. He first expressed this conception of a German-dominated, autarkic central European economic sphere in the summer of 1912, he brought it up again in the first week of August 1914, and he later reiterated it in a December 1915 speech, describing the new German economy as a “closed commercial state.”[118]

Historians who follow Fritz Fischer have understood this continuity as proof of German responsibility for the outbreak of the First World War. In this reading, Rathenau’s desire for a central European customs union—drawn up by Bethmann Hollweg on September 9, 1914, as point number four of his “individual war aims”—was a naked power grab akin to the simultaneous annexations demanded by Germany’s steel cartels.[119] The persistence of Rathenau’s vision of a unified European bloc and his willingness to make territorial concessions in a future peace suggests, however, that he saw war as an opportunity to carry out—albeit by force—the political integration necessary to counter the future economic power of the British, Americans, and Russians.[120]

In 1915, Rathenau described the creation of the KRA as an improvised, ad hoc institution existing in a regulatory vacuum, telling Swing, “there were no laws providing for it; there were no precepts for its conduct.” Furthermore, to make Germany economically self-sufficient Rathenau saw only three sources of raw materials: the occupied territories (to be “shorn of their supply of materials needed in Germany”), imports (“through the few doors left open to Germany”), and new “untapped” sources, such as substitutes, but also scrap or other materials “lying in obscure hiding places” ready to become “war grist.”[121] At no point did Rathenau address the possible illegality of these actions—that requisitions beyond the immediate needs of an occupying army contravened the Hague convention, that seized material from occupied Belgium transiting across Holland violated Dutch neutrality, or that the confiscation of German citizens’ private property had no domestic legal precedent. At the same time, Rathenau created a system that maintained the illusion of legality both to appease international opinion and, perhaps, to convince himself that these activities were simply (better) business as usual. This system, dubbed by Swing as “the Rathenau Plan,” formed the basis for what commentators would later describe as German “rational ruthlessness.” Although Rathenau admired the Prussian officer corps, his system derived less from military necessity and more from the economic imperatives of a civilian industrialist.

Through mid-August to early September, 1914, it seemed—as Rathenau had hoped—that Germany might be able to rely primarily on imports of raw materials from neutrals and on requisitions from occupied territory.[122] He had reason to be optimistic. As McAuley later noted, the British blockade was not impenetrable.[123] Vast imports of American copper, Australian zinc, Dutch East Indies tin, and Norwegian nickel passed through Italy, Switzerland, Holland, Denmark, and Sweden.[124] Possehl’s Lübeck became a hub for the Baltic trade, its harbor choked with vessels flying Danish, Norwegian, or Swedish flags and bearing cargo from the Americas.[125] “German money talks Esperanto,” mused an American correspondent, “its language is persuasively intelligent to all adjacent neutrals.”[126] Even after the blockade tightened, resourceful smugglers brought in metal clandestinely, including one cargo from New York seized in Copenhagen on January 1, 1915, containing a shipment of copper and brass disguised as decorations for German Christmas trees.[127] A month later, Dutch police discovered that smugglers with German help had been secretly warehousing copper scrap from throughout the country and then stowing it on boats headed up the Rhine.[128]

Requisitions from newly occupied territories promised even greater quantities. Though Belgium had no significant natural deposits of metal, Belgian foundries and smelters accounted for a large percentage of the world’s copper and zinc refining.[129] The same held—though in smaller quantities—for the refineries of occupied northern France, which produced 94% of French copper and 77% of French zinc.[130] According to Article 53 of the 1907 Hague Convention on Land Warfare, an occupying army had the right to seize “all movable property belonging to the State which may be used for military operations.”[131] The treaty, however, limited requisitions of private property to arms, munitions, and means of communication or transportation, and in these cases, the occupying army had to restore or compensate individual property owners after the war. That meant that any metal contained in government depots could—according to a very generous interpretation of the article—be legally confiscated, but stocks in private factories certainly could not. In the treaty negotiations in 1907, the German military representative had protested the stipulations on private property, citing “military necessity” and giving the case of the Franco-Prussian War when the Prussian army had to carry out extensive requisitions.[132] In the end, the German delegate voted for the article and approved the limitations, but German military planners ignored them when revising their regulations for the rear and occupied zones, the Kriegs-Etappen-Ordnung. The dry, technical manual specified that “the entire activity of the rear must be directed to relieving the field army,” and that relief included collecting “food, tools of all kinds, motor vehicles, factory and construction materials . . . beyond the current needs of the army.”[133] When Rathenau first approached Falkenhayn, he assumed that AEG could be supplied by requisitions from enemy territory. His assumption shows that this interpretation of requisitions was not limited to the German military.

Preparations for these requisitions began immediately. On August 15, Moellendorff noted in his diary that “foreign” stocks of copper, tin, and rubber had been discovered. That day he and his colleagues drafted an alphabetical list of the most desired categories of raw materials and established a new department for this plunder (the term they used “Beute,”—that is: loot, booty, or the spoils of war). To maintain control over the supply, they set up warehouses in Darmstadt, Munich, and Frankfurt an der Oder and outsourced the work of sorting and storage to a private firm.[134] After new information came in about supplies from Verviers and Antwerp, the Prussian War Ministry gave the KRA permission to send experts to Belgium to requisition the material, and then prevented the Ordnance Office from sending their own delegation there, maintaining that “the KRA solely [was] responsible for raw materials at home and abroad.”[135] By the following January, the KRA’s offices in Brussels had grown from two officers to a Raw Materials Office [Rohstoff-Verwaltungs-Stelle], that liaised with raw material committees set up in the areas behind the front in northern France and southern Belgium.”[136] From the fall of 1914 to the spring of 1915, German agents of the KRA scoured ports, depots, and factory storerooms in occupied Belgium and France, requisitioning substantial quantities of raw materials for immediate export to Germany. Their activities may have provided 80% of the German army’s needs for the first three months of war.[137]

Many of these requisition agents were German Jews: powerful bankers, industrialists, businessmen, and wholesalers excluded from entering the Prussian officer corps but particularly qualified for this task.[138] For example, the civil secretary of the AOK of the Seventh Army, stationed in Laon—a city in occupied northern France—was a German Jew named Moritz Meyer. Before the war Meyer had been the director of the Comptoir Métallurgique et Industriel in Paris, and general agent of the Mannesmann factory.[139] In some cases, these agents had very good intelligence on the industrial stocks. Many of them arrived with dossiers compiled from pre-war industrial espionage detailing the kind, quantity, and quality of industrial production.[140] They had also acquired much of this information legally through the files of the Schimmelpfeng Institute, a German credit information bureau with several branches in France. This commercial enterprise, partially funded by German Chambers of Commerce and the German government, provided information on the credit-worthiness of businesses. The branch offices each had boxes of index cards with detailed records on individual businesses by location and by sector (commerce, industry, agriculture, mineral production and extraction). Such files included the number of employees, general expenses, the mode of construction, production figures, brands of machines, the wholesale and resale price, and the raw material needs. With the declaration of war in the summer of 1914, the French government shut down the Schimmelpfeng offices in Paris, Lyon, Marseille, and Bordeaux, but probably not before the German War Ministry in Berlin had received copies of these confidential files and used them to determine the availability of raw materials in occupied territory.[141] In 1917, the New York Times branded this practice “robbery as a science.”[142]

In Belgium and northern France, the activities of the KRA—with their methodical agents armed with pre-war intelligence—need to be distinguished from the widespread, disorganized plunder of the German armies that preceded them. Each of the six armies that invaded northern France controlled the areas behind their front lines. These areas, known as Kriegs-Etappen or “rear areas,” were administered by the “rear echelon administrators” [Etappeninspektionen] of each army.[143] When an army first occupied a city, such as Lille in October 1914, the troops provisioned themselves with food, wine, clothing, and material goods from shops and homes, sometimes giving in exchange a hastily written “requisition notice.” These pieces of paper, as many diarists noted, were rarely reimbursed and usually completely worthless.[144] Some soldiers simply scrawled “good for a thousand kisses” or “receipt payable by Poincaré.”[145] In October 1914, on orders from the German military high command, the Quartermaster Generals of each army set up “economic commissions” [Wirtschaftsausschüsse] and passed decrees to organize and control these initial requisitions.[146]

The first stage of industrial requisitions proceeded sporadically over the course of several months. Beginning in October 1914, German military authorities in occupied Belgium blocked all sales of “war raw materials” [Kriegsrohstoff]—including copper, brass, lead, and zinc—and they requisitioned so-called “mass goods” [Massengüter], all merchandise “sold individually, or by weight and measure, [and] found in a considerable quantity by a single owner.”[147] These industrial requisitions began in occupied France in the late fall of 1914, though their timing and scale depended on each occupying army. On December 4, 1914, factory owners and wholesalers in Lille who sold “raw materials and mass-produced or manufactured goods” had to provide a list of their stocks.[148] Two days later, and 150 kilometers to the south, a diarist in Laon noted that Germans were “collecting copper, from the machinery of sugar factories to the instruments of the municipal music school.”[149] In early January, in Avesnes, under a different army administration, troops collected copper from abandoned houses and inventoried all industrial copper, bronze, brass, and tin stocks; by early March they removed truckloads of copper pipes from breweries and factories.[150] The variation resulted from the legal regime that all German requisitions fell under, with the exception of Bavaria and the General Governments in Belgium and Poland. In all other German territory, the KRA legislation relied on the Prussian Law of Siege of June 4, 1851, that divided civil executive power among twenty-four army corps districts each ruled by its own deputy commanding general. On the front, the actual commanding general of each district led his respective army corps and also wielded considerable executive power. Thus, despite a desire for centralized implementation, the KRA relied on a “group of dictatorships” to implement its decrees, often with “tragicomic” results.[151]

Rathenau went to considerable lengths to ensure that these requisitions had the semblance of legality. In many cases, the most efficient way to transport material requisitioned from the occupied territories was to ship it by water through neutral Holland. But the Dutch government rightly understood this transit as a violation of their neutrality and so prevented the shipments. To solve this problem, as a New York Times report revealed in February 1915, the KRA put in place the following procedure to make the transactions look like exchanges between private individuals:
After the goods are confiscated at Ghent, Antwerp, Charleroi, or Brussels, an emissary of the raw material department comes to the Belgian owner and makes arrangements for forwarding the goods. He then declares he is ready to treat about prices. If the owner agrees to invoice the goods to the company named by the emissary a better price is offered; if not the goods are taken away and the price is fixed by a commission in Berlin. Companies such as is suggested that the goods be sent to exist for all kinds of requisitioned articles. For wheat and other grain there is a company in at Mannheim, for leather a war leather company, and so on. They all pretend to be private companies. In reality, however, they are only a different name and mask for the Ministry of War. The capital is supplied by the Government and the profits or losses are accounted to the State.[152]
The reporter showed how Rathenau used companies like Kriegsmetall AG to make these clearly illegal confiscations look like legitimate trade—within a continental European economic sphere. The reference to the price commission refers to legislation in place after December 1914 that limited how much could be paid for certain critical raw materials. In late 1914, Rathenau managed to acquire additional funds to allow the Kriegsmetall AG to bring in more imports, “in every way possible,” by paying well above these official prices.[153]

As Swing’s article suggests, Rathenau felt no need to hide the KRA’s requisition policy—even though it flagrantly violated the Hague Convention. In his December 1915 speech where he described the origins of the KRA, he boasted about this requisition system to his audience: “We had to force all available materials beyond our borders into the country, however they were to be forced, whether by purchases from neutrals or by recovery in occupied countries.”[154] Later in the speech, he explained that British “terrorism on land and at sea” had caused purchases to subside, but “the violence of German arms” had opened up the rich resources of Belgium, northern France, and Russia. The KRA’s task then, was “to lift up these treasures and make them useful and yet preserve law and order, maintain oversight, and not destroy the countries’ economies with a single blow.” In order to do that, the KRA built on its “existing experience” and “penetrated” the occupied country with organizations and branch offices, concluded agreements on transshipments, arranged payments of customs duties, and gained access to railway lines. All these difficulties could be overcome, Rathenau claimed, because they no longer “took place on the unsteady grounds of unexplored economic conditions and legal relationships.”[155] For Rathenau, the perceived legal vacuum of wartime had allowed him to create his continental European economic sphere—one that had all the trappings of legitimate trade. The strongest evidence that Rathenau sincerely believed in this system was his creation in the spring of 1915 of the Reich Compensation Commission [Reichsentschädigungskommission] that provided a means for foreign owners of seized property to make claims and be reimbursed after the war—a provision of Article 53 of the Hague Convention.[156]

In his December 1915 speech, Rathenau failed to mention the most important component in his pre-war customs union scheme: Germany’s Austro-Hungarian ally. On July 31, 1914, before the declaration of war and mobilization, the German government had passed legislation that had strictly controlled the export of raw materials, banning exports not only to hostile countries but to allies as well.[157] This had left Austria-Hungary completely dependent on neutrals such as Sweden or Italy for imports. Even worse, Germany’s ban on transit of goods across German soil forced Austria-Hungary to rely solely on the Adriatic port of Trieste for maritime trade.[158] These restrictions had immediately caused severe shortages, especially for copper, 90% of which had to be imported.[159] But events in mid-September convinced Rathenau that such policies, without regard to the weaker ally, alone would spell disaster. After the French stopped the German advance at the Marne river on September 10 and the Russians routed the Austro-Hungarian army at Lemberg (later Lvov, now Lviv) the following day, putting Russia in control of Austria’s northeastern province, it became clear that Germany needed to prepare for a longer war.[160] The KRA began by economically reinforcing its Austro-Hungarian ally.

In late September, as German and Austro-Hungarian troops swept through western Poland, Rathenau joined negotiations in Berlin between representatives of the Prussian War Ministry and leading industrialists from Austria-Hungary. An agreement on September 24 resolved the immediate customs and trade issues. The Germans encouraged the Austro-Hungarians to increase their supply of raw materials from “foreign sources” (either through imports or from occupied territories), but in the case of copper, it was clear that they would still be dependent on Germany. The Germans required the Austro-Hungarians to compensate each delivery of German copper or brass metal by shipping half its weight in Austrian copper or brass scrap to refineries and foundries in occupied Belgium. And to meet those quotas the Germans suggested that the Austrians follow the German model for controlling their metal supply.[161] During the negotiations, the Austrian industrialists had some time to observe the workings of the KRA. On September 20, they held the first meeting of the Metallzentrale AG, a joint-stock company directly modeled on the Kriegsmetall AG.[162] The supervisory board soon began negotiations with Germany over the quotas of metal seized from Belgium and western Poland, occupied on September 28.[163] The stage had been set for further cooperation. An imperial ordinance on October 10 gave the Austrian government the power to intervene in economic matters and to impose fines and imprisonment for infractions.[164] The September 24 agreement did have a clause allowing for the eventual unification of both war economies, but no further action was taken beyond individual arrangements for specific raw materials and foodstuffs. Nevertheless, if Rathenau had hoped that these negotiations would lead to a customs union, he was soon disappointed. Delbrück did not support it, fearing cheap Austrian imports and believing that Germany had no interest shackling itself to such a weak partner.[165]

From mid-August through November staggering amounts of metal had flowed into Germany and Austria-Hungary through imports, smuggled goods, and requisitions from occupied territory. Yet even these quantities could not compensate for the unprecedented material losses in trench warfare. The statistics were sobering. To manufacture either three grenades or 150 bullets required one kilogram of copper.[166] A single bullet contained 10 grams of copper in its cartridge case; a single 82mm shrapnel shell contained nearly 2 kilograms of copper in its cartridge case, tube, fuse cover, socket, and driving ring. A battalion of 1000 men could fire, according to one estimate, one million rounds in a day of combat. Even if four-fifths of the shell casings could be recovered and recycled, the daily loss would still be 2 to 12 tons of copper. Applying the same estimate to field artillery meant a loss of roughly 500 tons of copper a day.[167] On November 14, Falkenhayn estimated that he had enough shells for four days of fighting at Ypres, and, indeed, the battle ended eight days later.[168] The German army had run out of shells. Fortunately for the Germans, so had the British, French, and Russians—the shell crisis was universal.[169]

With the severely constrained supply of metals and voracious demand, prices rose exorbitantly.[170] From June to the end of November 1914, the price of lead increased one-third, the price of copper two-thirds, the prices for both tin and refined zinc more than doubled, and the price for aluminum tripled.[171] Compared to prices quoted on the London metal exchange, nickel in Berlin was double, aluminum triple, and antimony and copper was quadruple.[172] On December 10, after a week of contentious meetings, the members of the Kriegsmetall AG managed to agree to a set of self-imposed price ceilings to control these soaring costs.[173] But the fixed prices meant that those who owned stockpiles of metal had no incentive to sell; subsequently, as the metal traders had warned, the law immediately paralyzed the trade.[174]

To prevent a complete shut-down of industry, Rathenau followed this legislation with a system of modified domestic requisitions. Rather than confiscate all the metal in the country, centralize it under state ownership in depots, and then redistribute it to hundreds of thousands of individual consumers, Rathenau realized that it would be much more efficient to leave the metal in place, allow property owners to maintain ownership, but give the KRA the sole right to allocate the metal.[175] Thus, the January 31, 1915, legislation enacting this system referred not to “requisitions” [Requisitionen] but “sequestrations” [Beschlagnahmen].[176] Austria-Hungary passed similar legislation one week later.[177] Though the Austrian February 7 decree used different terminology—“lay claim to” or “take charge of” [in Anspruch nehmen or übernehmen]—the confiscation procedure mirrored the German legislation, replacing arbitrary, case-by-case confiscations with a nation-wide obligation to report existing metal stockpiles and to allow the metal to be centrally allocated.[178] In both cases, property owners would be compensated and violators could be subject to fines.[179]

Taking over the role of the metal traders, the KRA determined the quantities of metal available in every metal consuming factory, sequestered it, and allocated it using a system of priorities that privileged shareholders of the Kriegsmetall AG and that responded to the shifting needs of military production.[180] To carry out the tremendous bureaucratic effort of registration and allocation, the KRA created three new subsections of Richard Tröger’s Metals Section: the Metal Registration Office [Metall-Meldestelle], the Metal Allocation Office [Metall-Zuweisungsamt], and the Metal Deallocation Office] Metall-Freigabestelle). The first two were headed by the Kriegsmetall AG board member Max von der Porten, ensuring tight cooperation between the KRA’s administrative structures and the metal industry.[181] Just as the KRA expanded its functions in the spring of 1915, so too, did its Austro-Hungarian counterpart. To collect the metal declared after February 7, the Austro-Hungarian War Ministry created a Central Requisition Commission [Zentralrequisitionskommission] within its offices in Vienna as well as Acquisition Commissions [Übernahmekommissionen] in Vienna, Salzburg, Graz, and Prague. Each commission had a representative from the local political, military, and financial administrations and a staff of experts.[182] The powers of the Metallzentrale AG increased as the need for metals extended beyond industry.[183] Though the Metallzentrale AG had been intended for metals throughout the empire, including Bosnia and Herzegovina, later in the war separate Zentrale were established in Budapest and Sarajevo.[184]

By the end of March 1915, Rathenau had carried out his vision of a central organization to control raw materials domestically, in occupied territories, and—by proxy—in Austria-Hungary[185] To everybody’s surprise, he chose to step down on March 31. Rathenau’s resignation has been interpreted variously as deliberate or forced; resulting from a decision to leave once the KRA could run on its own, or as a consequence of repeated personal attacks from business, political, and anti-Semitic circles.[186] In many ways, both seem to be true. In his correspondence, Rathenau indicated several times that he did not intend to keep the position for the duration of the war. He probably hoped to use the KRA as a springboard for a political career, particularly in the Finance Ministry. Thus, he identified a potential successor in the late fall of 1914 and completed the organizational work by the early spring 1915. At that time, however, Rathenau discovered that Bethmann Hollweg had decided to nominate his rival—Karl Helfferich—as minister of finance. Being passed over for the position left Rathenau bitterly disappointed. He saw the slight as more evidence that he would never be fully accepted as a Jew. “As a private citizen and a Jew, I have volunteered to serve the state,” he wrote to a friend the following year, “both of these groups cannot forgive me for it, and I do not think that this opinion will change in my lifetime.”[187] During his tenure at the KRA, Rathenau had come under increasingly hostile attacks from business associates who bridled at the state intervention, from politicians who resented the dominance of AEG and other big business in KRA war contracts, and from antisemites who saw him as a Jewish “war profiteer” whose Kriegsgesellschaften put the German middle class at the mercy of “international Jewry.” The Prussian officer corps remained a markedly antisemitic institution, and many, like former Prussian minister of war Karl von Einem, distrusted the “Jewish influence” that Rathenau represented; in a diary entry in late February 1915 von Einem even described the KRA as being full of “hooked noses.”[188] Nevertheless, Rathenau’s immediate associates in the Prussian War Ministry supported him, requested that he remain, and when he insisted on leaving, encouraged him to keep his position as commissioner on the board of Kriegsmetall AG.[189]

Only after his resignation did Rathenau’s work at the KRA became known to the broader public. The Prussian War Ministry, sensitive about state secrets and the prominence given to a civilian Jew, said very little about Rathenau’s responsibilities, both during and after his tenure.[190] The origins of the KRA only became widely known on October 11, 1915, when the Times of London reprinted Raymond Swing’s profile of Rathenau with the comment that it was “not yet too late to put at least some of the internal direction of the War Office into the hands of one of our English Rathenaus.”[191] This propaganda coup convinced many in Allied countries that Rathenau’s work had “broken” the British blockade, prompting widespread coverage throughout the British empire, the United States, Switzerland, and Germany.[192] Rathenau delighted in the attention. Moltke sent Rathenau a copy of the Times article; another friend translated it for a Swiss paper.[193] The successful reception of Swing’s essay encouraged Rathenau to speak publicly about his activities. On December 20, 1915, at a meeting of the Deutsche Gesellschaft 1914—an intellectual club that Rathenau had recently founded —Rathenau gave an extended speech about his eight months in the KRA that closely followed the story outlined earlier by Raymond Swing.[194] The following year the Prussian War Ministry published a transcript of the speech, but censored the two passages where Rathenau spoke about requisitions from occupied territory.[195] An Allied report on German exploitation noticed the censorship, found the original passages, and provided the elision as clear evidence of a centrally orchestrated “Rathenau plan” that not only exploited Belgian and French raw materials for the German war effort, but also stripped Belgian industry of its productive capacity in order to permanently eliminate Germany’s economic competitors for the postwar period.[196] Such a plan never existed, at least under Rathenau’s tenure. Nevertheless, the timing of these articles and publications meant that Rathenau became personally associated with policies—such as the systematic exploitation of private property from homes, businesses, churches, and government offices—over which he no longer had any control.[197]

— “Give it up, the army needs it!” —


Rathenau’s successor, Major Joseph Koeth, brought the KRA more in line with the ethos of the Prussian War Ministry.[198] Koeth, a South German and devout Catholic, began his career in the Bavarian Army and studied in the Bavarian War Academy, but was denied promotion to the Bavarian General Staff, apparently because his superiors saw his “philosophical inclinations” as an “unnecessary distraction.”[199] Highly ambitious, Koeth transferred to the Prussian army in 1899 and he joined the Field Artillery Division of the Prussian War Ministry in 1910. Called to the western front on August 1914, he fought in both the Marne and Champagne campaigns. At the time of the shell crisis in early November, Koeth returned to Berlin to develop a more efficient way to produce ammunition. At that time, he met Moellendorff and Rathenau, both of whom recognized Koeth’s potential as a future leader of the KRA. Though a career soldier, Koeth’s “philosophical” bent made him sympathetic to Rathenau’s ideas.[200]

When he took the helm in April 1915, Koeth soon discovered that Rathenau’s organization had failed to meet the increasing German needs. A survey of German factories, completed by May 1915, showed that Germany had only 3000 tons of nickel, 4000 tons of aluminum, 4500 tons of tin and 90,000 tons of copper, roughly a three-month supply given that the army had consumed 30,000 tons of copper in October 1914.[201] Koeth concluded that Rathenau’s “private-economic-businesslike outlook” that privileged industry and trade had contributed to the KRA being seen as “an irritating appendage of the War Ministry.”[202] In order to subordinate the KRA to military authority, Koeth created a central section—a true “economic General Staff”—that drafted business plans, took central control over personnel, handled legal questions, and displaced the thousands of ad hoc, case-by-case decisions that had characterized Rathenau’s organization.[203] Emblematic of this new “militarization” was Koeth’s decision to begin a program of systematic metal requisitions from Germany’s civilian population through the Metal Mobilization Office [Metall-Mobilmachungstelle] set up on May 1, 1915.[204] The choice to describe these activities as “mobilization” [Mobilmachung] signaled to the German population the need for sacrifice. Just as soldiers had been called to the front, so too had physical possessions.[205] In the spring of 1915, these possessions included all unnecessary—or replaceable—objects containing copper and nickel. Before the war, Germany had imported 200,000 tons of copper every year to produce industrial machinery, electrical and transportation infrastructure, and household objects. The KRA estimated that these objects in Germany contained 1 to 1.5 million tons of copper. “Mobilizing” even a fraction of this material—Levy’s touted scrap metal reserve—would meet the needs of the German military for the duration of the war, though at a great cost.[206]

The metal mobilization drives built on earlier voluntary conservation efforts. In late 1914, some municipalities had begun collecting household garbage, using kitchen scraps to feed livestock and saving bones for fertilizer.[207] Nation-wide textile collections—blankets, scrap wool, shoes—began in early 1915, often with the support of the Red Cross and local schools.[208] The first of these collections targeting scrap metal began in December 1914 when an unnamed “engineer” in Berlin suggested that a benevolent association chaired by a committee of Prussian nobles encourage a nationwide collection.[209] The Association for War Emergency Metal Collections [Metallsammlung gegen Kriegsnot]—with branches in all major German cities—called on school children and clubs to collect “foreign money, coins, gold and silver objects, tin, lead, copper, tin foil, bottle caps, tubes, and brass.”[210] Proceeds benefited wounded veterans and donations worth more than 10 marks earned an iron ring with an iron cross. The collections continued through April 1915 and brought in significant quantities of metal. In Berlin, one ton of metal had been collected in the opening weeks, and for the next two months schools and scout troops brought in regular truckloads of copper, zinc, and brass to the main collection center on Unter den Linden.[211] School children in Freiburg collected enough copper and nickel objects to fill three train cars.[212] Illustrated newspapers prominently featured photographs of children collecting objects.[213]

On March 11, 1915, thirteen-year-old Elfriede “Piete” Kuhr described her collection efforts in her diary, viscerally capturing her conflicting emotions:
At school a new collection is announced. This time, it’s back to copper, also tin, lead, zinc, brass and scrap iron. Gun barrels, guns, cartridge cases and so on will be made from it. . . . I swept the house from the very top to the very bottom. . . . I took old spoons, knives, forks, pots, kettles, a tray, a copper bowl, two brass candlesticks, antique belt buckles, a pan, several clothes hooks . . . Grandmother clapped her hands and shouted: “. . . Give up instead your lead soldiers, instead of taking my last belongings!” So my little army, with which Willi and I had so often played, had to die. In order not to sacrifice all the soldiers, I drew lots for the death row inmates. The one to whom the lot fell, I took out of the row of four and put him aside. When everything was decided, I grabbed the unfortunate ones by twos, put them in a giant tin spoon, and held it over the gas flame. The heroes in their pretty blue uniforms melted to death for the fatherland . . . Dreadful! I melted every last one. I wanted to cry. Instead, I laughed with my brother.[214]
Piete’s observations reveal how the “mobilization” of metals—billed as a way to build morale for the war effort—could indirectly encourage the opposite. The pain felt by Piete would soon be felt by her grandmother, when her precious metal objects fell under the KRA requisition order.

Private requisitions began on July 31, 1915, with the requirement to declare all household and commercial objects made of copper, brass, and nickel.[215] What the former KRA employee Ernst Kaeder described as a “ruthless action against private property” had been prefigured in earlier legislation that gave the KRA more control over requisitions and restricted the use of non-ferrous metals to military production.[216] The July 31 law targeted the entire population—private homes, religious and public institutions, businesses, dining establishments, lodgings—and, as Mary Ethel McAuley would later point out, the law targetted the largest metal objects in households: oven doors, hot water tanks, and large kettles used for washing clothes. As with the earlier “sequestration” orders in industry, the law required the owner of objects to declare their goods with written documentation, but that did not necessarily mean immediate confiscation. Any infraction of the legislation—false declarations or hidden objects—merited fines, and even imprisonment. To avoid the onerous inventory reporting requirement, individuals could voluntarily bring objects to collection centers, where they would be compensated by the kilogram. If they had already removed any non-metal fixtures like wooden handles or iron lids the compensation price would be greater.[217] Later that fall, McAuley took a photograph of a man who appears to be doing that kind of removal work; standing in front of a collection center, he knocks off the handles of a wash kettle with a large hammer.[218]

The KRA left implementation of the July 31 regulation to Germany’s 1200 municipalities. Each city hall had a Military Affairs Office that carried out military requisitions such as the blankets, oats, or canned goods required under the August 4, “War Powers Act.” To handle the sweeping demands for metal requisitions, the city government of Berlin—representing over two million people—created its own Metal Office that set up collection centers throughout the city in early August. The enthusiastic response took the organizers by surprise. “The rush during the first few weeks was quite extraordinary,” said Ernst Kaeder, “and it was further increased because the suburbs [of Berlin] had not initially created their own organizations, and so their inhabitants rushed in droves to the Berlin collection.”[219] Because of the demand, authorities extended the time limit for the voluntary submission several times until finally closing the collection on October 16. After that time, the Berlin Metal Office collected 150,343 declaration forms and began confiscating those materials in November and December 1915. In the end, as Kaeder explained, “the result of voluntary deliveries exceeded the expropriations twofold” and the people of Berlin gave “not much less than 3449 tons of metal,” mostly consisting of copper and brass.”[220]

Patriotism cannot explain all the enthusiasm; economic incentives also played a role. In mid-October 1915, shortly after the close of the voluntary submission period, Princess Evelyn Blücher von Wahlstatt, the English wife of a Prussian aristocrat living in the Silesian countryside, noted in her diary,
It is an interesting sight, though, to see cartloads of old pots and kettles and candlesticks, door-handles, chandeliers, etc., being driven along the street, and a poor woman or schoolboy carrying a copper kettle or brass lamp to the collecting offices to be weighed and paid for. For the Government pays well, and many people are tempted to be quick about getting rid of old copper for a good price, I heard a man the other day say that if he could get 100 tons of copper to sell, he would be a rich man for life![221]
Blücher’s observation pointed to a paradox of the metal mobilization programs. Though they enacted—to the letter—Norbert Levy’s pre-war solution for solving Germany’s raw material crisis, they did so by displacing the traditional metal trade. As head of the Association of German Metal Traders, with its 150 members from throughout Germany, Levy still officially represented that industry. The maximum price regulations and requisition legislation—policies that Levy had helped enact as a board member of Kriegsmetall AG—had “completely paralyzed” the activities of his association’s members.[222] In May 1915, the association successfully appealed to the Reich Interior Ministry to overturn the price ceiling legislation, but the July 31 mobilization made it increasingly difficult for them to operate.[223] Their sources of scrap metal dried up as their suppliers discovered they could illegally turn in industrial scrap to the collection centers and be reimbursed at rates well-above those fixed for the official metal trade.[224] The professional metal traders soon found themselves increasingly displaced by the labor of women, children, and opportunists—such as the man whose boasting Blücher had overheard.

If the traditional metal trade had been cut out, who processed the collected metal? In June 1915, the Kriegsmetall AG made arrangements with a private company to process the projected 60,000 tons of metal expected from six months of requisitions. Because of the extreme difficulty (and cost) of finding skilled labor to identify and sort nickel from copper and copper alloys, the company proposed that a triage center be established at a centrally located prisoner-of-war camp. There, skilled laborers drawn from the prisoner population, such as engineers and metallurgists, could do the work under military supervision. One unresolved worry of the organizers was the vulnerability of these objects to theft—particularly by those who worked at collection centers or helped transport the material in uncovered railway cars.[225] It was a legitimate concern. Already during the earlier voluntary metal collection, a Berlin man working at a collection center had been arrested and sentenced for 6 months of prison for stealing valuable coins.[226] Though the July 31 legislation clearly indicated the fines and imprisonments, an addendum signed by Koeth in mid-August ordered a strict police surveillance of all collection centers to control theft and prevent any trade in industrial scrap.[227]

On September 23, 1915, Austria-Hungary followed Germany’s lead with legislation authorizing the Metallzentrale AG to undertake private requisitions of copper, brass, and nickel.[228] Unlike the KRA, however, the Metallzentrale AG decided to emphasize the voluntary nature of the drive and support it with an extensive propaganda campaign.[229] In April 1915, as the War Emergency Metal Collection was coming to a close, the Austrians began a similar voluntary effort dubbed the “Great Patriotic Metal Collection.” It built on earlier, voluntary collections including one organized by “Austrian youth” as well as one offered to wine growers, an exchange of copper objects for allocation of copper sulfate—a fungicide necessary to protect the grapevines.[230] When the Austrians recognized the need for requisitions akin to those in Germany, rather than emphasize the punitive nature of future confiscations, they increased the propaganda for the “Great Patriotic Metal Collection” and made it clear that any donations would be exempt from future requirements to declare objects. The propaganda campaign included posters featuring a silhouette of soldiers manning a 30.5 cm mortar and the slogan “Our army needs metal!,” a film explaining the process by which the metal objects were transformed to munitions, and even a special Punch and Judy show for children.[231]

By late 1915, in both Austria and Germany, preservationists and connoisseurs realized that the population’s willingness to sacrifice was destroying priceless artistic, historical, and even architectural treasures. In Austria, experts scoured the piles of metal for valuable antiques, preserved 3000 of them from destruction, and displayed several of them in an exhibition in Vienna to promote the collection drive.[232] Similarly, in Germany the Märkisches Museum and the Museum of Ethnology purchased metal art objects salvaged from the collections.[233] Regional commissions of academics, religious authorities, and museum curators did on-site assessments of collection centers throughout Germany to save as many of these objects as possible.[234] Such concern, however, had its limits. Preservationists in Germany and Austria-Hungary could do little to prevent the removal of decorative copper roofs, begun in Germany shortly before McAuley’s arrival in the fall of 1915. On August 24, the KRA issued a confidential regulation—complete with reporting forms—that allowed city governments and religious institutions to remove, “voluntarily,” any “roofing, gutters, drainpipes, lightning rods, or window flashing made of copper or bronze.”[235] McAuley noticed that many cities complied, writing “In some cities the people willingly gave up the copper roofs . . . [i]n Berlin the roof of the palace, the cathedral and the Reichstag building are of copper, and in Dresden the roofs of all the royal buildings are of copper.”[236] Though the Reichstag kept its roof, a building near the American Consulate in Berlin was not as lucky, losing its copper roof in December 1915.[237] In Berlin, a total of 203 municipal and 394 private buildings lost their roofs, providing 351 tons of copper by the end of the war.[238] In Freiburg, “the municipal crematorium, several elementary schools, city hall and the theater” suffered the same fate.[239] From 1916 to 1918, the Austro-Hungarians removed 2830 tons of copper roofs.[240]

The Allied press and foreign observers saw these requisitions as evidence of weakness. Early in the war, the French government acquired a copy of Possehl’s 1912 speech predicting economic disaster for Germany. It was translated, published, and immediately disseminated to neutral countries as a response to German claims of economic self-sufficiency.[241] An American returning from Berlin in November 1914 told the Daily Telegraph that “lack of copper and gunpowder” meant that Germany could not last until June; the London Times seized on Germany’s first requisition orders on January 31, 1915, to predict that copper shortages in Germany and Austria-Hungary augured imminent defeat.[242] The following October, Evelyn Blücher annoyed her fellow German dinner guests—including Rathenau’s rival, Finance Minister Helfferich—when she “ventured to suggest that the scarcity of copper might bring about an earlier end to the war.”[243] That December another British woman living in Germany wrote to her sister,



My dear Emmie . . . I have just been reading the morning’s list of forbidden things—no Christmas cakes may be baked, no milk may be served in any restaurant, shop, hotel, or pension, and all copper and nickel has to be given up by the 31st March—the last pleased me most of all—it looks as if they had not the enormous supplies of copper and nickel for ammunition that we have been led to believe.[244] The American ambassador to Germany, James Watson Gerhard, came to the same conclusion in February 1916 when he noticed that “copper lightning rods of churches [had] been taken and an effort was made to take a brass reading desk in the American Church and the fittings in the Japanese Embassy.”[245]

The October 11, 1915, publication of Raymond Swing’s glowing portrait of Rathenau, however, caused many British and Americans to question this commonly held view. One of Swing’s readers, a homemaker in London, wrote in her diary on that day, “I have just read an article from an American paper which has made my heart sick with apprehension. For the first time, I ask myself whether the German race with its genius for invention is meant after all to have the mastery of the world.”[246] Rathenau himself noted that the obsessive British press coverage of the article suggested that it had left many “depressed,” an attitude only reinforced with the widespread positive coverage of Rathenau’s December 1915 speech at the Deutsche Gesellschaft 1914.[247] McAuley’s columns for the Pittsburgh Dispatch from 1915 to 1917 also reflected this view of German resilience.

Müller’s numbers show that the collection of copper and nickel succeeded. Metal allocations—except for lead and tin—peaked in 1916-1917 then declined the following year. The only metal allocation to decline from the opening of the war to August 1916 was tin. Because the statistics did not include company reserves and did not specify where or how the metal was acquired, other figures must be used to assess the relative importance of imports, domestic production, and mobilization. In the case of nickel, by December 1916, German Silesian mines had produced an estimated 1275 tons and Norwegian mines under German financial control 1400 tons.[249] Requisitions and purchases from occupied territories had provided at least 858 tons and the metal collections contributed 250 tons.[250] In the summer and fall of 1916, the cargo U-boat Deutschland’s two blockade-breaking voyages to the United States brought over 400 tons of Canadian nickel as well as over 150 tons of tin.[251] Increased nickel production, both in Germany and Norway, supplemented by more intensive metal collections, ensured the nickel supply for the duration of the war.

For copper and its alloys (brass and bronze), the metal collections provided a more substantial percentage of the total allocation. By August 1916, the KRA had acquired 69,528 tons through requisitions and purchases from occupied territories, an additional 6441 tons through purchases from neutrals, 2771 tons from German copper roofs, and 55,914 tons from the metal collections.[252] According to Max von der Porten, over the course of the war, the metal mobilization brought in 90,000 tons of copper, which accounted for nearly 1/3 of the copper allocated during the war.[253] Much of the remaining increase after 1915-1916 was due to the Austro-German-Bulgarian invasion of Serbia on October 4, 1915, and Germany’s takeover of the Serbian Bor copper mines, which had produced 7000 tons of copper annually before the war.[254] The precipitous decline in copper allocations after 1916-1917 is deceptive. After 1916, the KRA began developing ways to substitute iron, lead, and zinc for “scarce metals” like copper: for example, replacing copper locomotive boilers, guiding rings, and even coins with iron. The redesign of shells and use of iron substitutes may have reduced the amount of copper needed in munitions production by 90%.[255] The procurement office for weapons and munitions [Feldzeugmeisterei] required 12,000 tons of copper a month in 1914, but only 5000 tons of copper a month by the middle of 1917.[256] The Austro-Hungarians experienced similar success with substitute products and the development of substitute materials.[257]

These statistics suggest two conclusions: first, the initial mobilization was successful, second, in the winter of 1915-1916 the KRA perceived the greatest threat to the German metallurgical industry to be neither copper nor nickel, but tin. Indeed, in a speech on April 15, 1916, Karl Nugel—the director of the German Association of Metal Foundries—admitted that he worried about Germany’s tin supply.[258] This supply had diminished primarily because Holland (under British pressure) had ended its limited exports to Germany the previous October.[259] By the fall of 1916, requisitions and purchases from occupied territories had provided a total of just 539 tons, purchases from neutrals only 445 tons. The metal mobilization contributed very few tin objects, the statistics compiled by the Kriegsmetall AG in 1916 did not even list the metal in its final calculations.[260]

Military developments on the western front contributed to the tin shortage. Demand for tin—required for the production of fuses—increased exponentially with the “second munitions crisis” in the summer of 1916. The immense material losses of the Battle of the Somme (July 1 to November 1916) convinced the German military high command to institute the ill-conceived Hindenburg Program: the order to double the munitions supply and triple the supply of machine guns and artillery by the spring of 1917.[261] Koeth opposed the program, but he soon found his authority at the KRA subordinated in a newly reorganized War Office [Kriegsamt].[262] The founding of the War Office on November 1 brought a new stage of “militarization” to the KRA. At its head was General Wilhelm Groener, who had been responsible for Germany’s railways during the mobilization of August 1914. Groener’s appointment consolidated military and political power; he simultaneously held the position of Deputy War Minister and a seat in the Bundesrat as a Prussian representative. A decree allowed Groener to issue orders to the Deputy Commanding Generals and their economic officers and a subsequent order created War Office Bureaus [Kriegsamtstellen] in each of their districts.[263] The following spring, the War Office also centralized the previously independent, uncoordinated collection activities of schools, women’s associations, and charitable organizations through a War Committee for Collection and Aid [Kriegsausschuß für Sammel- und Helferdienst].[264]

Beginning in the spring of 1916, the Germans and Austro-Hungarians carried out extensive requisitions of metal objects made of tin and their alloys (such as pewter).[265] These included the pewter lids for beer steins—the university-town Freiburg collected 800 kilograms—and, more controversially, in January 1917, the tin organ pipes in German churches, including 26 tons alone from churches in Berlin.[266] In 1917, school children throughout Germany collected 220 tons of tin cans, though with a tin content of just 1/10 that meant only 22 tons of the actual metal.[267]

The only significant quantities of tin left in Germany and Austria-Hungary were in objects made of bronze—an alloy of tin and copper— and the largest bronze objects were statues (10% tin) and church bells (15-20% tin).[268] Beginning in early 1916 the Metal Mobilization office began targeting public monuments, such as Freiburg’s bronze statues. Church bells quickly followed. By the end of 1916, several churches in Germany voluntarily sacrificed bells, especially those cast after 1890.[269] The following spring, in the Baltic city of Rostock, parishioners protested against the March 1, 1917, decree requisitioning bronze church bells. They argued (correctly) that the metal from their twenty-nine bells would not actually be used for the war effort given the two years’ time needed to remove the bells and transform them into munitions. Their entreaties fell on deaf ears. Even though an expert designated several of the bells as historic monuments in April, all but two were removed the following June.[270] Similarly, shortly before McAuley left Berlin in July 1917, she described how a friend of hers attending mass learned that the church bells would be destroyed:
Before the service opened and just as the priest mounted the pulpit the church bells began to ring. When they had stopped the priest announced that this was the last time the bells would ever ring, for they were to be given to the metal collection. The people began to cry as the priest went on, and before he had finished, many were sobbing out loud. Even the men wept. My friend said that it was the most impressive thing that she had ever witnessed.
Berlin churches sacrificed 99 tons of bronze church bells.[271] Removals like these were repeated in thousands of cities and towns across Germany. In the summer of 1917, just one single firm processed 70,000 such bells; in January 1918, a Prussian cultural official estimated that “half of all church bells in Germany had been committed to ‘higher service for the Fatherland.’”[272] In Austria-Hungary—after May 22, 1917—the Metallzentrale AG requisitioned similar numbers of church bells. By the end of 1917, they had removed nearly all the large church bells in the empire and had begun “to gather school and small table bells.” From 1915 to 1918, they collected over 13,000 tons of bronze church bells, accounting for nearly half of all copper alloys (including brass and bronze) Austria-Hungary acquired during the war.[273]

In the summer of 1917, borrowing from Austro-Hungarian precedents, the KRA instituted a propaganda campaign to encourage voluntary participation. Unlike the “Great Patriotic Metal Collection,” however, German propaganda efforts were woefully inadequate. For one, they reinforced the perception of forced requisition of valuables through an ill-chosen iconography. The poster featured a muscular, unsmiling, brute with a working cap, overalls, and a work shirt with rolled up sleeves. His powerful forearms held open a large sack, bursting with copper and bronze objects, including pots, chains, storefront lettering, candelabras, coat racks, fire pokers, and even a small ornate bronze statue. The slogan was not an exhortation to participate in a voluntary, collective action, but rather, a direct, forceful order: “There is enough aluminum, copper, brass, nickel and tin in the country! Give it up, the army needs it![274]” At least in their propaganda the Austro-Hungarians had the decency to say “our army.”[275]

Arguments in the written propaganda only buttressed fears that the metal drives were a measure of desperation. “It is not a sign of shortage,” read the top of one pamphlet, thereby associating the collection and shortage, despite the negative qualifier. The pamphlet then claimed that Germany’s “strength” in metal included its abundance of bronze bells, statues, and cannons, copper roofs, lightning rods, and stills—all objects that had fallen under KRA and Kriegsmetall AG requisition orders. In smaller print the pamphlet gave rational counter-arguments to typical objections, noting that copper roofs built before 1850 were spared, that church bell bronze contained valuable tin, and that the piles of unused goods in the collection centers (due no doubt to transportation difficulties) constituted valuable reserves.[276] The following year, a March 26, 1918 decree extended the requisitions to an additional list of 123 objects, including door knobs and latches, samovars and coffee machines, railings and fences, and curtain rods.[277] Rather than create new propaganda material, the KRA reissued the “give it up, the army needs it” poster with a slightly modified text. In bold, red, letters, the slogan read, “Home furnishings made of aluminum, copper, brass, nickel and tin, have been appropriated, give them up now!”[278]

The integration of collection activities into the War Office administration in the spring of 1917 made it easier for the KRA to mobilize the propaganda potential of school children, already active in voluntary collection drives of used paper, fruit pits, thistles, human hair, gramophone records, and coffee grounds.[279] At the end of November 1917, local authorities in Cassel held a conference to train teachers about “patriotic elementary school teaching.” A professor at the University of Frankfurt acknowledged the sacrifices required to salvage needed raw materials: “The consumption of metals is immense . . . so we must voluntarily separate ourselves from our beloved mementos, so that we do not leave relatives standing out there without a weapon.”[280] Following his talk, the local leader of the Collection Aid Committee [Sammelhilfesdienst] gave the assembled teachers their marching orders:
After returning to your homes, you have been asked to carry out this educational work . . . Each child must come to the following awareness: “Through these activities, in my own small way, I help to defend my homeland and not only do I sing ‘Deutschland, Deutschland über alles,’ but, through this action, I show that my fatherland is valued and loved by me, [that] I am a combatant in the struggle against Germany’s enemies.”[281]
Not all the children got the message. In one collection drive organized by the Red Cross students stole the metal, sold it on the black market, and used the money to buy sweets and go to the cinema. In other cases, gangs of youth stole copper gutters, telegraph wire, and even all the light fixtures from a restaurant.[282] By the end of the war, theft was made even easier because brass door knobs and latches had all been removed and replaced with wooden ones or string.[283]

Though the German population resented all of these measures, door knobs and latches was the only removals to be opposed by a vote in the Reichstag, partly because of Koeth’s successful lobbying efforts. The first extensive political opposition to the metal requisitions came on May 7, 1917, when the Reichstag discussed issues related to the controlled economy. The confiscation of church bells—ordered just two months previously—had particularly upset the Center Party’s delegates, who could be relied upon to represent the interests of the churches and had been inundated by letters from their constituents. Deputy Lederer, a Bavarian Catholic, gave an extended speech arguing that the measure be delayed. He pointed out that metal sequestered in July 1915, in some cases, had yet to be delivered; that metal remained in shut-down breweries, distilleries, textile factories, and properties owned by German aristocrats; and that even the Reichstag chambers still had their brass chandeliers, copper coat stands, and bronze statues. He dismissed the argument that Germany’s “Austrian brothers” had allowed their bells to be removed with little protest by saying that the Austrians had remained calm because the Austrian character was “lighter.” After expounding on the aesthetic and religious character of the bells, he argued that the compensation paid for the metal was far too low, reimbursed less per kilogram than even “a sooty wash kettle” and he demanded additional guarantees for funds to replace the bells after the war. Koeth met this impassioned speech with a sober, calculated explanation of the need for the measure, then closed with an Austrian poem lamenting a village bell being sent to the front and appealed to the delegates’ patriotism: “[Our people] will understand how the metal, which once rang so seriously, now goes into the field, as a bright weapon, high in the air in an airplane, in a submarine below the water, crashing and bursting as a bullet into enemy lines, in order to pave the way for victory to our brave musketeers.” The delegates erupted into lively bravos and the measure did not pass.[284]

By the summer of 1918, public opinion in Germany had turned decisively against the war.[285] On July 4, the metal requisitions came up again at the Reichstag, with with legislation proposed that would prevent the removal of door knobs and latches from private homes until their removal from all public buildings.[286] The deputy who sponsored the legislation, Otto Arendt, explained that before Germans sacrificed their door handles, he wanted the removals to be “carried out in the countries occupied by our troops,” claiming it “self-evident that what is imposed on the German citizens as war necessity . . . must be endured by the inhabitants of the occupied territories.”[287] Arendt’s perception of a more lenient treatment of the occupied populations, however, was not his primary motivation for wanting to revise the legislation. Rather, he feared its effects on Germany’s own public order. Angry homeowners, incensed at the implementation of the regulation, at the inadequate compensation, and at the inferiority of replacement latches, had already held public demonstrations.[288] These protests threatened to grow into concerted actions of what Arendt appropriately called “passive resistance”; homeowners intended to paralyze the War Office by flooding it with appeals for individual exceptions to the regulation.[289] Whereas Lederer’s speech a year earlier had only resonated with his fellow Catholics, Arendt’s fiery words were interrupted variously by cheers from the Social Democrats, the Catholic Center Party, and Arendt’s own German Party—a new merger of several antisemitic members with the Free Conservatives [Reichspartei], to which Arendt (himself a converted Jew) had belonged.[290]

Koeth again responded with measured arguments, namely, that the country needed all the metal from both public and private buildings, that the total amount of statue metal left in the country was only 2500 tons (just one week of production), and promised that the government would cover the costs of replacement latches after the war. Furthermore, he insisted that Germany’s enemies had not experienced any special treatment. “Every effort is made to extract the ‘scarce’ metals from the occupied territories,” Koeth said, “and if you come to Belgium, you will find that the doorknob regulation has already been carried out.”[291] But this time Koeth failed to mobilize any remaining patriotic sentiment in his listeners. Fearful of the resentment that the requisitions might provoke, and of the political consequences of public disorder, the delegates voted the next day 151 to 85 to pass Arendt’s measure — —and German doors remained knobbed.[292] Just four months later, the discontent presaged by opposition to the doorknob decree erupted in open rebellion that overturned the monarchy and established a republic.[293]

After the signing of the armistice, on November 11, 1918, Koeth remained at the KRA in Berlin, now the head of an economic Demobilization Office charged with undoing the entire system that he had helped put in place.[294] When he finished that work in the spring of 1919, the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace had begun commissioning authors for its volumes on the economic and social history of the war. Because of his key role in the KRA and his continued prominence in the new, Republican government, they chose Koeth to write a volume on “The Supply of Raw Materials under Government Control.” The projected work, as announced in a 1924 prospectus, envisioned not so much a summary of the “well-known scheme originated by Dr. Rathenau for meeting the effect of the blockade,” but rather, a study of the KRA’s impact “upon the economic structure of Central Europe.” Instead of a strictly “military history” the volume intended to shed light on “the effect of the War upon the normal economic life of the countries concerned.”[295] Koeth, however, never wrote the volume. Though he briefly served as the Economics Minister for a short-lived Stresemann cabinet in 1923, stayed on the boards of several companies, and occasionally advised the Reichswehr—the small German military organization allowed by the Versailles treaty—Koeth soon withdrew from public life and spent his final years in complete retirement before his death in 1936.[296] The task of drafting the Carnegie volume fell, instead, to Otto Goebel, a professor of Political Economy, whom Koeth had first met in December 1915 when he joined the KRA as a member of a Scientific Commission tasked with studying the KRA’s inner workings.[297]

Whereas Koeth had refrained from taking political positions, Goebel became a supporter of the Nazi Party—signing a “vow of allegiance . . . to Adolf Hitler and the National Socialistic State” in November 1933.[298] His history of the KRA, Deutsche Rohstoffwirtschaft im Weltkrieg einschliesslich des Hindenburg Programms (1930), therefore, was colored by his antisemitism and fierce German nationalism—he admired Koeth’s leadership for “reining in the large percentage of Jews in the raw material administration that Rathenau had brought in.”[299] Rather than carry out the aim of the Carnegie Endowment for Peace of exploring the impact of German requisitions on occupied Europe, Goebel did the opposite. In his analysis of the metal mobilizations, he focused solely on the German experience, where 19 million households and 40,000 churches and religious institutions had been affected.[300] “It was millions of individual cases of seizure,” Goebel acknowledged, “the largest sources were from industry—electrical equipment, distilleries, breweries, sugar factories—and from consumers: town halls and other public buildings, hotels and inns, churches, schools, monasteries and individual households.”[301] But Goebel prefaced this discussion with a striking claim: “The war revealed the strange and perverse fact . . . that the subjects of the country at war were more strongly affected, in every area, than the inhabitants of the militarily conquered and occupied territories. In the occupied territory we mobilized stocks with great restraint and tried repeatedly to purchase instead of putting in place requisitions.”[302] Given the recent research on German exploitation of occupied Europe, it would be tempting to understand this statement as simply the delusion or misrepresentation by a Nazi sympathizer writing in the 1930s. Yet Goebel expressed a commonly held view (in Germany) that was not completely without merit. In some cases, German administrators—much like the Reichstag delegates in the summer of 1918—attempted to soften the impact of requisitions in order to maintain public order. Even Shotwell, the editor of the Carnegie Series, claimed in his 1940 attack on “the German way of war,” that “Allied public opinion has never done justice to the efforts of these non-military administrators of the occupied territories,” men like Moritz von Bissing in Belgium, whose “chief difficulties were with the generals of his own army.”[303] Documents in German, Polish, and Belgian archives broadly confirm Shotwell’s conclusion. Nevertheless, occupied areas under direct German military control certainly did not experience any of the “restraint” described by Goebel.

In occupied Belgium, Governor-General Moritz von Bissing tried to moderate the degree of economic spoliation in Belgium to maintain order. He may have been further motivated by the desire to prepare for Belgium’s “future annexation”—Rathenau’s project for a central European economic sphere—or to counteract Allied propaganda that used German atrocities in Belgium to rally support for the war.[304] Even before his arrival at the end of November 1914, Rathenau’s KRA had difficulty convincing authorities in Belgium to hand over raw materials. On September 14, one of the KRA delegates sent to Brussels reported that unspecified “present conditions” had made “fruitful work impossible.”[305] Rathenau later complained that “theoretical concerns” for Belgian industry made it difficult to survey the stocks of Belgian factories.[306] In a meeting about Belgian financial contributions in December 1914, Bissing clearly expressed his position that he saw the country, “not as a lemon to be squeezed,” but rather, as a viable economy that could be directed towards German war production. The general representing the German high command replied curtly, “each drop not squeezed from the Belgian lemon, must be squeezed from a lemon, or better yet a pumpkin, from Germany.”[307] In the spring of 1915, Bissing created an “Economic Commission” with representatives from the German civilian and military administration in Belgium as well the KRA to control increasing KRA requisitions.[308] When the committee met in Brussels on June 19, 1915, Bissing reiterated his views about the role of the Belgian economy for Germany: “I think . . . that a squeezed lemon has no value and that a dead cow can no longer give milk.”[309]

Nevertheless the KRA kept squeezing and slaughtering. Even though Bissing continued his steps for administrative autonomy, the KRA took over the acquisition of raw materials in Belgium following the institution of the Hindenburg Program and the creation of the War Office in the fall of 1916.[310] At the end of that year the German authorities began extensive requisitions of copper, lead, and nickel.[311] From a single Belgian factory, Vieille-Montagne, by the end of the war, the Germans had requisitioned 42,000 tons of zinc, 4000 tons of lead, 18 tons of silver, and 1543 tons of sulfuric acid.[312] After the war, a Belgian observer described his memory of the copper requisition in Mons, recalling the lines of impoverished citizens who delivered their precious objects to the Germans, only to watch them be destroyed:
Poor emaciated people, in rags . . . they carry in their arms or transport in carts the most precious possessions of their household: the old wash basin, all dented, encrusted with a thick crust of soot. . . the small lamp that lit the long laborious winter evenings; the tender bric-à-brac, our slum-dwellers’ poor luxuries: the copper chandelier, . . . [the] bronze [statue] that decorated the chimney—can one know the value of these things?. . . Miserable, they go with a heavy, resigned step, a stoic expression, silent: they have suffered so much since the first days of occupation that they have lost the force to complain . . . A large handcart, piled to its rails with a sumptuous cargo of chandeliers, lamps, girandoles . . . perched on the vehicle, two soldiers buried to their knees in metal, stomp it, crush it forcefully beneath their heels, with a sort of voluptuous hatred.[313]
By the end of the occupation, the Germans had dismantled distilleries and breweries for their copper stills and fermentation tanks, removed the street lamps of Brussels (each one containing only three kilograms of metal), torn out underground copper electric cables, cut off the bronze decorations on the greenhouses of the Botanical Garden, and taken the bronze Franco-Flemish composer Roland de Lassus off of his pedestal.[314]

But the KRA did not remove the church bells. The credit is often given to Cardinal Mercier, who encouraged Belgian Catholics in passive resistance to German demands.[315] In letters to Bissing, to Germany’s chancellor, Count Georg von Hertling, a Catholic, and even to the Vatican, Mercier protested the requisitions from private households as well as from churches, but particularly of organ pipes and church bells.[316] Yet similar entreaties by the Catholic hierarchy—and papal intervention—in northern France and Russian Poland went unheeded. In occupied Belgium, the attitude of local German authorities made the difference. Bissing agreed with Mercier’s demands and argued successfully to his superiors to spare the bells and avoid widespread public protests.[317]

Occupied Poland is often cited as another case, like Belgium, where a governor-general—in this case General Hans von Beseler—attempted to soften the economic deprivations of the German military.[318] This moderation was initially seen in the case of metal requisitions. One reason for the leniency may have had to do with distinguishing the German occupation policy from that of the former Russian rulers. When the Russians retreated from Warsaw, according to Evelyn Blücher’s German brother-in-law, they “killed all the animals and removed everything out of the houses, even down to the brass handles on the doors; everything that might be of any possible use to the Germans.”[319] That also included church bells “sent to Moscow,” as well as “the smallest copper and brass fitting in workshops or private houses.”[320] Thus instead of beginning with forced requisitions of non-ferrous metals, the Germans began with voluntary purchases—a policy they applied even more extensively in the occupied Ober Ost, where they even relied on Jewish middlemen for their purchases.[321] After passing a decree blocking the metal, Beseler’s government set up metal collection centers to accept donations.[322] Beseler later decreed that after August 1916, those who had not voluntarily given up metal objects would face a fine or imprisonment. He also extended industrial and household requisitions to match similar measures in Germany (boilers, distilleries, oven doors, and roof decorations). Yet the forced requisitions that had been threatened never materialized. Instead of increasing compulsion, Beseler raised the prices paid for the metal and reduced the maximum fines and prison sentences to 5000 marks and one year in prison.[323] These policies were successful. Up to mid-1916, Beseler’s General-Government exported 70% of Poland’s raw materials to Germany.[324] By October 1916, purchases from the entire occupied eastern territories (including both Poland and the Ober Ost), provided a total of 13,360 tons of metal, more than double the total in occupied Belgium and France: 9358 tons of copper and its alloys, 1189 tons of zinc, 1702 tons of lead, 373 tons of aluminum, 301 tons of spent munitions, 79 tons of nickel, and 25 tons of tin.[325]

As in Belgium, however, the KRA eventually began to take over more economic control. In February 1917, a deputy general officer in Berlin explained that the “Two Emperors’ Proclamation” that created a new “sovereign” kingdom of Poland on November 5, 1916, had not changed the economic situation.[326] Indeed, following Beseler’s decrees on April and November 1917, economic commando teams working for the KRA removed bells, roofs, and housing fixtures.[327]

Beseler may have modeled his policies after those in the Austrian occupation zone. Shotwell claimed that the conquered territories that provided the most resources for the Central Powers were “in parts of Poland and in Serbia—where the Austrians in occupation did surprisingly well.”[328]

In occupied Serbia, Romania, and Italy, however, it is clear that the Austro-Hungarian and German soldiers proceeded with requisitions as extensive as those in Germany. A postwar report on the spoliation of Serbia listed 7320 tons of copper kitchen utensils from private households, 300 tons of copper stills from peasant farmhouses, and 7692 tons of copper objects.[329] These requisitions, which began in late 1915, proceeded in the same fashion as in Belgium: mass requisitions from the stocks of factories, shops, as well as abandoned houses, followed by requisitions of copper objects from households—“brass heaters, cauldrons, platters . . . doorbells, door knobs, bedsteads and fireplace grills”—, and finally churches, including “brass chandeliers, lead and copper roofs, bells, vases and ornaments.” All but one of the church bells in Belgrade was removed. By the end of June 1917, foundries in Vienna and Budapest had received over 107 tons of bells. Stiff penalties faced those who did not declare their objects.[330]

Romania and northern Italy experienced the same pattern but within a more concentrated time-frame.[331] Beginning in December 1916 teams of soldiers from the German and Austro-Hungarian occupation army—under the pretext of searching for copper and other metals—combed through Romanian villages, house by house, now removing also soap, cooking oils, eggs, linens, and other provisions.[332] From December 1916 to March 1917, the German economic office [Wirtschaftstab] in Bucharest exported 29 railway cars of scrap iron, 15 of assorted metals, 6 of lead, 4 of iron, and 1 of copper. By the end of the war, the occupation forces had removed 31,017 tons of scrap iron—including torn out railway lines—2670 tons of copper (kitchen utensils, pots and pans, lamps), and 1924 tons of bronze, including 500 tons of church bells.[333] In addition to that, exports from Romania included an additional 53,129 tons of metal.[334] In the fall of 1917, German and Austro-Hungarian troops in occupied northern Italy took copper objects, lightning rods, copper roofs, and—with the aid of four specially trained squads of engineers—church bells. Organ pipes from Italian cathedrals provided 5 tons of tin.[335]

The economic exploitation of Romania provided a model for “rational ruthlessness,” studied by both victims and perpetrators. An economic office—Wirtschaftsstab or “economic staff”—was first established on October 19, 1916, by the German high command. A German major and an Austrian colonel provided the leadership and their divisions systematically covered all aspects of economic life: finance, applied geography, foodstuffs and fodder, war raw materials, petroleum, agriculture, forestry, labor, shipping and clearing, and machinery reclamation.[336] A study of its operations published during the Third Reich described it as creating “a system out of chaos, an orderly economic complex from an expanse of rubble. The resolution of this problem was of extraordinary importance for the further development of the war.”[337] A Romanian biologist at the Natural History Museum in Bucharest concurred. When asked by a German about the effectiveness of the economic office in occupied Romania, he replied: “I am a biologist, but, in all of the animal kingdom I do not know of a parasite that has an apparatus for sucking blood as perfect as that developed and perfected here by the Wirtschaftsstab.”[338]

— Conclusion —


The “parasitic apparatus” seems far removed from the “German Sparsamkeit” celebrated by young Mary McAuley, yet it belonged to the same economic structure. When it came to economic exploitation during World War I, it is undeniable that there was a “German way of war.” But, as has been shown, it cannot be reduced to arguments about “military necessity” or German character. Understanding how and why the Germans developed the system of “rational ruthlessness” that they inflicted on occupied populations and their own citizens must acknowledge the peculiar geopolitical situation of wartime Germany. As a resource poor country fighting a “battle of resources” [Materialschlacht] under the effects of a British blockade, they had very few alternatives but to systematically exploit all the material resources of the continent through a central organization.

This solution was not—as Fischer and his followers might have it—planned in advance. It was an improvised response, led initially from experts in industry and, for metals, drawing heavily on Jewish expertise. In the first eight months of the war, the KRA was built through the work of engineers influenced by the latest American efficiency experts. In the occupied territories, German businessmen turned soldier, including German Jews, identified and requisitioned sources of raw materials for German industry, doing so, as much as possible, under the guise of legitimate trade. Rathenau and his fellow civilian organizers paid little regard to international law, seeing it as an impediment for their projects, and justified their activities as a legitimate response to an illegal British blockade. When Rathenau’s planned custom union incorporating Austria-Hungary into a central European economic sphere failed, he used economic constraints to force Germany’s ally to adopt a parallel, and subordinate, economic organization.

In the spring of 1915, the Prussian War Ministry took the first steps towards integrating the largely civilian institution of the KRA into its military structures. If there was a moment that “military necessity” became a justification for KRA operations, it was with the appointment of Major Koeth. The creation of the “Metal Mobilization Office” led to drastic interventions into the private sphere, bringing with it a parallel shift in the ethos of the institution as civilian engineers and economists gave way to Prussian soldiers. Increasingly invasive requisitions, begun in Germany, soon followed in Austria-Hungary, and then in the occupied territories. In her study of the German home front, Anne Roerkohl described how those who did not give up objects voluntarily could expect “the invasion of their households by city officials, policemen, or soldiers, followed by court action and large fines.”[339] The same applied to everybody living in Europe on territory controlled by Germans or Austrians. The real material needs of the army—particularly for tin—meant more and more sacrifices of increasingly valuable civilian objects. These sacrifices and the harsh means used to enforce compliance built resentment. As we have seen in Germany, it harmed morale and threatened public order, erupting in protests in the summer of 1918.

How did the occupied populations experience these deprivations? How did their resentment manifest itself? What were the consequences? In most existing accounts, which center on the actions of the German occupier, the impact of these requisitions on the inhabitants of occupied Europe has been ignored. The Belgian civilians watching helplessly as German police crushed their precious copper goods could stand in for the millions of similar cases throughout Europe; but even that depiction oversimplifies a much more complicated reality. What consequences did such violence have on the subsequent behavior of occupied populations? Were forms of passive or active resistance to these measures successful?

After all, those German police did not act alone. In the case of Romania, the famed Wirtschaftsstab, as recent research has shown, owed its success less to its systematic organization and more to the incredible degree of local cooperation it found among Romanian elites such as Grigor Antipa, the author of the Carnegie Endowment volume on the economic exploitation of his country.[340] The process of identifying, collecting, sorting, transporting, melting down, and refashioning metal objects into munitions required the support of an extensive networks of professionals—labor that could not solely be provided by Germans and Austro-Hungarians. What motivated somebody in an occupied country to work on behalf of the KRA? To begin to answer these questions, we now turn to a case study of occupied northern France.
