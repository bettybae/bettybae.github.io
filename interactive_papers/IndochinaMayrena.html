The Mayrena Case


In December 1887 the Jarai having looted a large convoy from the mission, Fr. Guerlach decides to punish these turbulent neighbors. In mid-February 1888, he appeals to all the Bahnar who have had to suffer the Jarai robberies; he then gathers 1200 warriors, at whose head he marches against the looters, who are routed, and, eight days later, come to make peace. This event maximizes the prestige of the missionary; never, in the memory of the savages, had they seen such a multitude obey one man and, especially, never had the dreaded Jarai been defeated. Also, the rumor spreads quickly that a lang (spirit) was living in the body of the missionary around whom the arrows had fallen without touching him and whose numerous troops had no one injured. Fr. Guerlach is from then on considered a living god whose power the Moi fear with terror, and his prestige is significantly increased; his influence, enormous over the Bahnar, extends to the neighboring tribes where he is feared and respected.

It was at this moment that will play the prelude to the Franco-Siamese fighting by the emergence of a man who has remained famous under the name of Marie I, King of the Sedangs.

Unscrupulous adventurer, but endowed with high intelligence, Baron Mayrena arrives, in April 1888, at the home of the Moï [people] of Binh Dinh; his avowed goal is the exploration of the gold sands of the Attopeu region, but he is, in fact, charged with a more or less secret mission by the French government, which has provided money and guides; he must, indeed, group under his authority the diverse independent small Moï tribes of the hinterland and only stop at a day’s journey from the Mekong; he is solely responsible, but if he succeeds, he will step down in favor of France. On 23 May 1888, he joins the Catholic mission of Kontum accompanied by Fr Guerlach, who went to meet him. The next day, they continue on to the Northwest villages and the hamlets crossed easily sign a treaty of submission; on the 28, Mayrena crosses the through the Pekô and happily makes similar deals with other the Sedang chiefs of the district.

Everything worked to perfection thanks to the support of the missionaries and, on June 3, Mayrena establishes the constitution of the Sedang Kingdom that several leaders signed the same day.

CONSTITUTION OF THE SEDANG KINGDOM

“Today, June 3, 1888, the leaders of all the territories that signed a treaty with Mr. Marie Mayrena and acknowledged him as supreme leader, met with the men of their tribe in the village of Kon Gung, and, after translation and explanation, have declared their acceptance of the following constitution:

“Article 1 – The independent territories that combine today, take the name of the Moï Confederation.
Art. 2 – The Sedang territories being the most significant in this Confederation, this [Confederation] will be called the Sedang Kingdom.
Art. 3 – M. Mayrena, already the recognized leader, was elected king of the Sedangs.
Art. 4 – The monarchy is hereditary; but the king, if he wishes, may appoint a successor outside the family. However, tribal leaders demand that the king be approved by all the leaders, with a majority of votes.
Art. 5 – The national flag will be solid blue, with a white cross with a red star in the center.
Art. 6 – The king has absolute authority: he has command over all civilian and military leaders and resolves disputes that may arise between them.
Art. 7 – He decides on war and peace, with the assistance of a council composed of the heads of the tribes.
Art. 8 – The king leads men to war or designates the person who must lead them.
Art. 9 – The land is only alienable [transferable to] foreigners with the consent of the Taoules and alienation [transfer] must be sanctioned by the king.
Art. 10 – In the future, human sacrifices are forbidden.
Art. 11 – All religions are free in in the Sedang kingdom.

Made in Kon Gung June 3, 1888.”

P. Guerlach countersigned and “testified that it had been well and duly signed by the high contracting parties.”
Finally, in an additional paragraph, he states that the Sedangs have, without coercion, accepted the kingship, and that M. de Mayrena, “only fired their rifles at the request of the savages, curious to test, without danger, the range of French arms.”


On June 20, Mayrena leaves to deal with the Hamong on the right bank of the lower Peko, as well as the Sedang-Reungao, the Hamong sign the Constitution of the new kingdom: a third tour, to the Sedang residents of Peko and Psi, achieves the same success. It is at this point that Fr. Vialleton, superior at the Kontum mission, succeeds in persuading the Bahnar villages to unite in a confederation, of which an influential Bahnar, Krui, was elected president by all the elders gathered in a plenary assembly in Kontum. The Constitution of the Sedang Kingdom, once signed, a treaty of alliance was concluded between Mayrena and this Bahnar-Reungao Confederation, which was going to be officially recognized by the General Resident Rheinart.

In September, de Mayrena returns to Quinhon and newspapers publish his constitution.

But then, the adventurer, become Marie I, King of the Sedang, intends to remain sole ruler of his new lands and no longer speaks of delivering them to France; even more, he spreads the rumor that he left the management of his territories to the missionaries, who are ready, he says, to take up arms at his first signal. At the same time, he discredits himself with a real scam and the adventure does not take long to finish in a lamentable and tragic way; his lies have, in addition, created around the missionaries, who had so generously assisted him, an atmosphere of doubt and mistrust. Treaties passed with the Sedang chiefs had been written and countersigned by Fr. Guerlach, who had concluded with the adventurer a defensive alliance against the Jarai. Mayrena had used all these documents to compromise the mission; he did not however have any difficulty to exculpate himself and Mr. Hénart, Resident General of France, then deciding to act, requests the assistance of the Resident of Quinhon, who will go to the Moi people, to make an act of authority in the name of France.

It is at the end of March 1889 that Mr. Guiomart, Resident of Quinhon, accompanied by Mr. Simoni and six Annamite militiamen, arrives at Kontum; at Kon Trang, had been convoked the Hamong and Sedang-reungao leaders, signatories of the Mayrena Constitution. The Resident informs them of the removal of the “King,” has them hand over the flags, insignias and proclamations distributed by the adventurer, at the same time he invites the leaders to join the Bahnar reungao Confederation, formed by the mission and whose president, Krui, was recognized by the French government. The leaders easily submit to this proposal and sign their entry into the Bahnar-reungao Confederation, which would only be dissolved in 1895, with the definitive establishment of our authority.

This act, prelude to our official installation in the hinterland, affirms already, facing the claims of Siam, our actual possession of all the lower basin of the Kr Bla and Kr Pekô, where Kontum stands in front of the Siamese avant-garde from Attopeu as the advance guard of our power, it is on this bastion that the efforts of our officers in the fight that opens against Siam for possession of the hinterland will find support; the courageous missionaries who, alone, occupy this point, will not stop offering our officers, along with the support of their material resources, the support of the great influence they enjoy with the evangelized Bahnar and over the the circumjacent tribes.

A special mention is to be made of a confederation of tribes united formerly under the command of a French explorer in the Sedang kingdom.

Mr. Marie de Mayrena, departing from the Lower Mekong under the auspices of Mr. Constans, who treated him like a spoiled brat, arrived at the beginning of 1887 on the borders of the Rongao-Jolong. Missionaries have been there for some fifty years, catechizing with moderate success, working, looking for the origin of these people, who they believe to be descendants of the Khmers, without having, however, a monument, an inscription or a book that could enlighten them on the past; the region seems to have no trace of antiquity, nor any ancestral inhabitants. According to one of their superstitions, the missionaries, while being well treated, cannot penetrate into the interior villages or families. M. de Mayrena would not have found a better reception than the missionaries and the previous explorers without using a ruse.

When a stranger arrives in the country, every village, which is fortified, closes and declares itself Diêng (sacred). Whoever breaks the Diêng is punished by death. The missionaries, like the others, are subject to this law. The Diêng was proclaimed before Mayrena. He declared that he cared little for the villages, but, having learned that a German mission from Europe wanted to subdue the Sedangs by force, he came to warn them and help them defend themselves. All the villages opened up before this savior; they provided him gigantic escorts. Arriving in the capital, Attopeu, he had behind him five thousand warriors delirium who cheered as if he had called for the revision of the Constitution. He was appointed king by these savages, and signed them a kind of charter. I do not know if the German mission has rendered many services to Prussia, but it was very useful for Mayrena.

So he built there a kingdom, a bit like Jules Gros in the hilarious Counani kingdom, only the consequences could have been more serious; he wielded for some time a real influence with all the barbarians, on a tract of country equivalent to that of our initial three provinces of Cochin China.

Once established Mayrena offered the sovereignty of his kingdom to France; he was sent by the Governor-General to Mr Le Mire, the Resident of Quinhon, who gave Mayrena carte blanche to spread his credibility. From then on, Mayrena suppressed the tricolor flag and replaced it with the blue flag with white clover, which was proclaimed the royal Sedang flag. The missionaries, who had strongly pushed him forward, abandoned him on this occasion, and the Protectorate categorically refused to support him.

To escape the financial difficulties that the creation of his kingdom causes a king without money, Mayrena went to Hong Kong, and tried to borrow money from the English, first on his good looks, and then on his iron quarries, then on the Sedang kingdom. Repulsed on all sides, he made, at the expense of the Bishop of Quinhon, a bill that gave him unlimited credit at a Chinese man in Haiphong, who sumptuously dressed the Sedang army, without ever touching a penny.


He was not the only one Mayrena awed. Firth the missionaries, then the commander in chief, then Resident Le Mire were all his dupes. At wits' end, he finally went to Paris, where his majesty was quickly completely uncovered; he has since returned to Bangkok, without any chance of success whatsoever.


With the amazing qualities of courage and initiative that distinguish him, Mayrena could have perhaps succeeded in his risky business, if his lack of delicacy had not alienated his natural protectors at the outset, and if he had not acted against the precepts of the most vulgar honesty, a quality valued even among savages, and whose previous dealings with the Atchin Sultan and the Saigon police demonstrate that he is completely destitute.

Attopeu has, however, since been visited not only by the father Guerlach, from the Bahnars mission, who made me aware of Mayrena’s odyssey, but by official visitors, who have been able to find that the influence of the king of the Sedangs had disappeared from these lands, and that the Protectorate had naturally replaced him.
